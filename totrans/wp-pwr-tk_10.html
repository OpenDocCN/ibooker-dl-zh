11 AI and search engine optimization

This chapter covers

  • Exploring the effect of AI on web searching
  • Choosing the best keywords
  • Asking AI to scan a site
  • Earlier AI search implementations
  • Taking advantage of Google Search Console
  • Using the AIOSEO plugin and sitemaps
  • Maximizing backlinks
  • Anticipating ways to optimize AI site ranking

Search engine optimization (SEO) used to matter. It meant modifying your website so that search engines such as Google or Bing would list your site up high in the results when people search for your site’s topic. This no longer works.

The goal was to get a link to your site listed at least on the first page of the list of links Google displayed. Few people looked at the links on the second page. This was what SEO meant in the past. AI is seriously disrupting SEO. Things are very different now. SEO is being replaced with AI optimization (AIO).

Google just displayed a list of links that you could visit to get the information you were after. Now, AI skips the links and just gives you the info. Some AIs, such as Gemini and Perplexity, include a list of links as citations. And you can request citations from any of the AI systems. But links are optional.

AI scans sites and reads their contents. Then, like a research assistant, AI provides you with the answer to your query. And if needed, you can chat with AI, asking for a different or more precise response.

Put simply, AI gives you answers; Google just gave you a list of places to look for answers yourself. Now it’s as if you had a researcher at your disposal and asked them to look around the internet, solve a problem, and then bring you the results.

Because AI is changing internet searching, you as a site designer must switch your efforts from SEO to AIO. In this chapter, you’ll see how to do just that.

We’ll also look at Google Search Console, an important resource to help you increase your audience. It can measure traffic and performance for your keywords, which are vital for content optimization. And, we’ll also explore the popular SEO plugin All In One SEO (AIOSEO), which includes a sitemap builder to make it easy for AI to quickly understand your site’s organization.

AI assesses the quality of your site somewhat differently from the algorithms Google and other earlier search systems used. AI critiques are more sophisticated and include larger data searches and more intelligent assessments. Soon AI will judge sites as well as an experienced human website critic.

That said, some previous SEO tactics (having good backlinks, especially from highly-ranked sites, for example) still work. Let’s take a look at how best to please AI.

11.1 Learning new optimization tactics

Google and Bing are rapidly incorporating AI into their search engines. Google is adding its AI alternative—Gemini—to its search page (a bit unsuccessfully so far). And it displays an AI Answer button you can click. Bing prominently displays its AI Copilot icon. And now, both search engines just answer questions when you search rather than displaying a list of links to make you look for the answer.

One thing, however, remains the same: your site will do best if you focus on providing the very finest content you’re capable of. That requirement will never change no matter how sophisticated AI becomes. Ultimately, quality should be your primary goal.

Decades of reverse engineering, hundreds of SEO experts’ tips, and instructions from Google itself have produced a huge store of advice about SEO. But what about AIO? What criteria does AI use when deciding which websites to visit and cite?

AI training involves, among other things, massive data scraping—roaming the internet and storing information. So, you provide a prompt, and it searches its huge existing data store. But AI is also increasingly gathering other data from YouTube videos and other multimodal sources. Most AI systems are now (or soon will be) capable of providing answers on current queries in real time. Do they use Google when searching?

Even though traditional SEO is being disrupted by AI, some tactics remain important for impressing AI systems. One such tactic is learning to include the best keywords.

11.2 Using good keywords

Keywords are the words people used to type into a search engine when starting a search. If, for example, they wanted to know the best place in Dallas to sell their coin collection, the keywords would be those in italics.

This keyword list could also include synonyms, such as shop, coins, buyer, and dealer. I wouldn’t include numismatic. Few people are likely to use that word, much less know how it’s spelled.

So, your first AIO task is to figure out what are the keywords for your site’s topic. Normally, your site’s title and tagline contain keywords. And you can prompt GPT:

I’m setting up a website for my coin shop in Dallas. I buy and sell. What are some good keywords for me to use in this site?

This book’s practice site’s title is Time Tested Recipes for the Instant Pot. And the tagline (the additional description right below the title) is Comfort food for beginners. So, what do you think are the keywords here?

Certainly, Instant Pot and recipes. After the obvious ones, you should consider abbreviations such as IP. And synonyms such as easy (for beginners) or pressure cooker or multi cooker (for Instant Pot).

If you’re at a loss for words, submit your site’s address to the Google Keyword Planner here: https://mng.bz/N1eX.

TIP In the early days of search engines, their analyses were largely quantitative. So, you could succeed simply by keyword stuffing—that is, use keywords as many times as possible. These days, keywords must be used naturally; they must belong in the content where appropriate. AIs are sophisticated, and their judgments are increasingly qualitative—much more like the way a human website expert would judge sites.

It’s crucial to carry out in-depth keyword research to find the most pertinent and important terms that accurately describe your content. Think about your target audience’s search intent before anything else. What topics are your likely visitors looking for? Then, what terminology would they use to find sites that would be useful to them?

Who are your visitors? What words would they use when searching? You should aim for a total of about five keywords—too many, and they become less effective.

Spend some time thinking about who your potential visitors are. What might they ask AI that would cause AI to cite your website? Jot down those keywords. Finally, go to an AI, and, using those keywords, see what sites are cited. Visit these sites. Are they similar to your site? If not, rethink your keywords.

Tip A free keyword tool from WordStream (www.wordstream.com/keywords) can give you some good ideas.

After determining which keywords are best, ensure you include them organically in your body text, headers, and other areas such as captions. Don’t force them into content where they don’t naturally fit. AIs can tell. Cramming your content with keywords will damage your AIO.

Instead, concentrate on producing excellent, worthwhile content that truly satisfies the search intent associated with the keywords you’ve selected. Think keywords that represent the topic and focus of your site. Use tools such as Google Analytics (see chapter 12) to track the effectiveness of your chosen keywords on a regular basis and make any necessary revisions.

11.2.3 Using long-tail keywords

Some say you should think about making keywords more specific by adding modifiers. Instead of just Instant Pot Recipes, you would add adjectives like this: Instant Pot chicken breast recipe.

Recall from chapter 1 that you were urged to focus your site’s topic. Instead of Fishing, you would attract many more visitors, and ultimately more conversions (repeat visitors), if your site’s topic is Bass Fishing in North Carolina’s Randleman Lake.

The same concept applies to long-tail keywords. These are longer, more specific keyword phrases. For example bass bait is a regular keyword, but bass lures for winter fishing is a long-tail version. This is one way to reduce your site’s competition.

If people intend to find what you’re specifically offering, then they might well type in some adjectives. Most search queries include adjectives, so long-tail prompts are likely to match your long-tail keywords. But do use your head. Keep in mind what you would look for. You wouldn’t type flavorful IP chicken breast recipes, would you? How many people would use flavorful in that query? They might, however, use best, popular, easy, or healthy.

You might be thinking: well, I’m writing sentences, so I just naturally use long-tail keywords in my site’s text content. True enough. But here are some ways to improve your long-tail efforts by bending your syntax a bit, or adding a modifier:

  • Go to Google, and type in your site’s topic. As you type, see what Google provides as suggested auto-completion. For example, if I type instant pot, I get this list of auto-completion terms: recipes, rice, chicken recipes, beef stew, pinto beans, and pot roast. Doing this might also give you some ideas about subjects you should consider including in your site.

Tip You can also always count on AI to let you know if you’ve left out any important topics. Here’s my prompt to GPT: “Scan all the pages in my website at https://watergarden.signalstreaming.com. Please look at this and respond by listing any topics I have not included that should perhaps be added to my site.”

  • Pay attention to how you word your links. While the site’s main menu item links should be quite short, other kinds of links can be more descriptive and thus include long-tail keywords. Again, moderation is key though. Don’t make them too long, or let them sound forced.

  • If you know any lingo or special terms used by your target audience, consider incorporating them into your keyword phrases when appropriate. Or ask AI what special terms or jargon are used by people cooking with the instant pot.

  • Check out this Moz keyword tool (https://moz.com/explorer). The site claims Keyword Explorer can provide more than a million keyword suggestions.

  • See what phrases your competition is using in their sites.

  • Many prompts and searches include a question. So, consider using some questions in your text. “What is the best IP recipe for pinto beans?” (Then, provide the recipe.) Q&A is said to be a good way to improve AIO. Also, use the AnswerThePublic tool (https://answerthepublic.com/) to see what questions are typically used related to your keywords.

  • Ensure that you provide thorough answers to any questions you raise. Depth and comprehensive content are appreciated by AI, search engines, and your audience. But also include brevity. Variety—some long answers, some short—is best. The length of your answers should be just enough to satisfactorily answer the question. Comprehensive and thorough don’t necessarily mean lengthy. Short can suffice: Is salt always used in savory dishes? Yes.

11.2.4 Improvements in ranking

For years, Google has continually modified and improved its site assessment algorithms. In fact, it’s estimated that Google makes hundreds of minor adjustments every year and major modifications about once a month. Major here means that there’s a noticeable effect on ranking.

Google has been using AI tools also. Google was using some of these methods even before AI arrived: deep data mining, contextualization (context sensitivity), and latent semantic indexing (LSI).

Understanding Google’s evolving algorithms and AI techniques is important for improving a website’s visibility and, for many sites, ultimately its popularity. If they don’t visit you, your site won’t grow. Website owners should stay as informed as possible about algorithm changes in AI systems. The following subsections describe the main AI procedures that Google developed on the road to full AI.

Deep data mining

AI systems specialize in being able to study large collections of data—far too large for a human to analyze and find hidden patterns. AI can hold massive data collections in its mind all at once. It sees hidden patterns, relationships, processes, and trends. They’re hidden from us humans, anyway. For example, by looking at all social media messages, AI could identify trends in food preferences long before they became widely known. What’s more, AI learns and improves itself over time.

Contextualization

Before exploring the specific terms that shape how search engines function, it’s important to grasp the fundamental concepts that drive their ability to understand and deliver relevant information. By understanding these foundational ideas, we can better appreciate how search technologies interpret user queries and prioritize meaningful results:

  • A study of meaning—Semantics, as it relates to search engines, is the study of how computers interpret words and their semantic connections. When a user types a query, search engines attempt to determine what they are actually looking for. Each letter typed in sends the search down a more specific pathway through the data.

  • Relevance—Worthwhile search results should make sense in relation to the user’s search keywords. Content that offers precise, in-depth responses or details on the actual subject matter of the search phrases will appear higher in the results.

  • Exclusive terms—AI is now quite good at deciphering the purpose of a query and the relationships between ideas. These days, AI goes well beyond simple methods such as keyword matching. AI looks for meaningful information as effectively as a human searching a big database, if a human could actually do that in a lifetime.

Latent semantic indexing

Latent semantic indexing (LSI) is a technique used by AI to understand the relationships between words and concepts, helping to provide context beyond mere keywords and improve the relevance and quality of search results:

  • Close content examination—AIs use the LSI mathematical technique to identify connections between words and concepts in a website. AI provides context and goes beyond just working with keywords.

  • Associated words—LSI determines words, phrases, and their relationships, that is, what terms are commonly used together. When a user searches for “right,” for instance, LSI can assist AI in determining if the user is referring to the opposite of left or correct.

  • Topical depth—AIs give preference to websites that offer a thorough grasp of a subject by mentioning a range of relevant terms. If you use synonyms and semantically related terms, AI will boost your score because it assumes you’re fully addressing the topic. And vice versa: if your site doesn’t cover an element important to the site’s topic, you’ll be downgraded.

  • Detecting keyword stuffing—Semantics and LSI-related strategies also help AI decide if you’re just repeating keywords for no purpose.

Tip Always aim to satisfy your visitors’ needs and expectations with the finest, most helpful content you can provide. AIs are now perfectly capable of noticing if you try any kind of trickery. So don’t be tempted to even try any LSI stuffing either. Offer clean, clear, useful info.

  • Advanced techniques—Complex neural networks, large language models (LLMs), and huge data sets now let AI systems understand both the intent of a prompt as well as the actual quality, appropriateness, and depth of a site’s content in satisfying that intent. So, you must present your audience with valuable content if you want to be approved.

11.2.5 Understanding Intentions

Experts describe several different intentions that you should consider when imagining the thought process of your prospective audience. User intent refers to the basic motive or objective of a user’s query. It’s important that you understand user intent specific to your site’s topic if you expect to come up with the right keywords. Satisfying user intent also helps AIO. User intent typically comes in four flavors:

  • Navigational intent—The user knows what they need. They want to go to a particular narrowly defined page or website. For instance, looking up “IP recipes” or “Randleman Lake fishing” demonstrates that the user is aware of their destination. Or they want to buy a particular kind of fishing lure or see a specific recipe.

  • Informational intent—The user wants information. For instance, a search for “how to tie a fishing knot” suggests the user is looking for guidance or knowledge.

  • Investigation—Before deciding on a purchase or course of action, the user is weighing their options and seeking additional information. For example, searching for the “best bass fishing lures” means they want to weigh their options before buying.

  • Transactional intent—The user plans to make a transaction, such as a purchase. For instance, they’re ready to buy a product or service.

When you match your content to the intent of your users, you increase the likelihood that your website will satisfy your audience—and AI and search engines love that. For example, a thorough, accurate, educational article that does a great job of addressing the queries of the majority of users searching for a particular term is probably going to rank higher than a product page.

Your content should be polished and fulfill the informational needs of your audience. For instance, if they are primarily looking for information about fishing methods, equipment, or nearby fishing locations, this strategy improves user experience and boosts AIO simultaneously, which results in more repeat business as well. 11.3 Employing Google Search Console

Google offers Search Console, a valuable collection of useful ways to—if not game their and AI’s evolving ranking systems—at least keep up with your competition. Google describes this set of tools like this: “Search Console tools and reports help you measure your site’s Search traffic and performance, fix problems, and make your site shine in Google Search results.” Be sure to visit the Google Search Console site (https://search.google.com/search-console/about) to learn more.

Sites are always in competition with other sites to some extent, and your search engine rank is one gauge of how well you’re competing. You can be sure other site designers are taking advantage of Google’s dedicated SEO and AIO tools.

With Search Console, you can improve and troubleshoot your website’s visibility in search results. The Console offers instruments and data to assess the performance and volume of a website’s search engine traffic, address problems, and maximize your visibility. You’ll find a number of utilities and reports, including crawling error notifications, search query analysis, and indexing status checks:

  • Crawling error notifications—These come to you by email when Google finds problems with your website. You’ll be told which URLs (both home pages and interior pages, each of your pages has its own unique URL) are affected by these problems, and then you can notify Google once you’ve resolved them. This kind of error includes server errors, redirect errors, and pages not found (404 errors). Put more simply, Google’s crawler bot was unable to read content in these pages because it couldn’t find them.

  • Search query analysis—Find out which queries (and therefore which keywords or keyphrases) are actually sending visitors to your site. You’ll be able to see your site’s position in Google’s search results.

  • Indexing status checks—These are similar to crawling errors, but these errors mostly happened after a page was successfully read by the crawler bot. Google crawled the page, but for a variety of reasons didn’t index (rank) it. An unindexed page’s content hasn’t been analyzed by Google, nor stored in its database, and thus becomes ineligible for display in the search results.

You obviously need to check periodically to ensure that your site’s published pages are being successfully indexed. Your site might not appear at all in the search results, or at least the content of that page won’t. True, a single 404 (missing page) error won’t necessarily exclude your entire site from being considered for listing in the results, but it certainly won’t help your rank. Fix broken links to nonexistent pages as soon as possible. You want to ensure that all your content is being indexed.

Frequent updating is also said to improve AIO. And if you do add or polish content, you can let Google know by submitting individual page URLs or sitemaps to the Console.

Definition A sitemap makes it easier for AI or Google bots to understand the types of content, their interrelationships, and their relative importance. This is particularly useful for large sites, or sites with lots of varying content. But every site should offer a map. This next section describes an SEO plugin which, among many other things, generates your sitemap for you, allows you to edit it, and then helps you submit it to Google and AI.

Get a quick SEO report

Try using a Google tool called Lighthouse to get a report on your site in the five categories shown in the following figure.

You’ll get a quick report on the quality of various elements of your site when you use the Lighthouse tool.

You’ll get a narrative report along with a score between 0 and 100 when you run a Lighthouse audit. Here’s what these categories mean:

  • Performance—This tests the site’s efficiency and tells you ways to speed up page loads. It includes metrics such as load time, interactivity, and other factors.

  • Accessibility—This is about navigability, particularly as it relates to people facing various challenges when using a computer. This includes screen-reader compatibility, Alt Text for image descriptions, and whether your pages contain a usable semantic structure (in other words, are you including HTML elements such as <nav>, <article>, and <section> to provide a clear outline of the various types of content). This is useful for AI and search engine crawlers, as well as allowing people using screen readers to decide which sections of a page to skip, and identifying the location of the next section.

  • Best Practices—This report checks for secure resource loading, correct use of HTML tags, and other best practices. If you’ve violated these practices, this part of the results will tell you where.

  • SEO—The SEO category ensures that the page is optimized for search engine ranking. It checks for correctly structured data, responsiveness, and other technical elements that affect both search engine and AI ranking.

  • PWA (Progressive Web App)—This category measures if the site is fast and reliable. It looks at offline functionality, responsiveness on mobile networks, and adherence to PWA best practices. This one doesn’t provide a score from 0 to 100 like the others.

Your report will grade each category, except PWA, and provides specific recommendations for improvement based on the audit results.

11.5 Employing backlinks

The early search engines made simple, primitive judgements about site quality. For example, they would just count how many times the word recipe appeared in a site. And the site with the greatest number ranked highest.

We’ve come a long way since then. All kinds of deep data searches, AI judgements, and subtle analyses are currently employed when assessing the quality of a site. Backlinks—links to your site from another site—are still considered an important factor. Backlinks are particularly valuable if the other site linking to your site is itself highly ranked. In effect, this means that the content of your site is respected by reputable site managers. They linked to your site. So, it’s likely that you’re doing a good job and offering easily accessed content of real value.

Of course, once Google introduced backlink ranking, various dishonest schemers popped up offering to sell you tons of fake backlinks—you pay them and they backlink to you multiple times from their array of automated link generators in zombie sites. It didn’t take Google long to figure out what was going on and to penalize the parties involved.

How can you get more true backlinks? It’s legit (well, maybe semi-legit) for you to reach out to a quality site with a topic related to yours. Then, offer to exchange links. You can also offer to provide guest posts on reputable blogs and include For Further Information backlinks in your post. Finally use a link gap analysis to look at the backlinks in sites that compete with yours. This gives you a list of sites that you might want to invite to link to yours.

Here’s a tutorial from Semrush that will assist you with gap analysis and additional ideas about how to obtain quality backlinks: www.semrush.com/blog/how-to-get-backlinks/. And here’s more assistance with this topic from Backlinko: https://backlinko.com/google-ranking-factors.

This is a brief checklist of effective ways to generate backlinks:

  • As always, make sure you have valuable content, unique if possible. This way, your site will naturally attract backlinks.

  • Go to conventions and network.

  • Join and participate in forums and other sites.

  • Use social media to spread the word.

11.6 A checklist of blunders

As you’ve seen, search engines are quite sophisticated by now—and AI is pushing the analyses further toward (and eventually beyond) human-level critical judgments of site value. By now, it’s probably become impossible to game AI site judgments. Don’t even try.

That said, let’s look at a list of obvious bungles you’ll do well to avoid if you want to impress AI. Some of these suggestions appeared in different chapters, but it could be useful to have them gathered together here in this single checklist:

  • Redundant, offensive, or inaccurate content.

  • Ugly or merely decorative images.

  • Broken links to nonexistent web pages, within or without your site.

  • Not doing keyword research.

  • A dysfunctional or unattractive site on tablet or cellphone screens—It’s true all good current themes do their very best to make every site responsive (look good when collapsed into small spaces). But every site represents its own unique challenges, so always view and navigate your site on small screens before making it public. And make any necessary adjustments so it’s effective and usable when shrunk.

  • Dodgy ploys such as redundant, forced, or overused keywords.

  • Discouraging search engines—WordPress.org (the hosted version of WordPress, not the one when you sign up with WordPress.com) has a setting that you need to look at. Go to your Settings > Reading screen and ensure that the Search Engine Visibility option is turned off. You don’t want AI or search engines to judge your site until it’s polished and ready for its closeup.

  • Poorly written content.

  • A stuffy, overly formal writing style.

  • Too much of anything—annoying pop-up ads, too many ads, too much animation, music or video that starts automatically. Practice moderation.

  • Pages with little text—Called thin content, it can reduce your score unless there’s a good reason for it. A good reason would be an artist’s portfolio of paintings or a catalog with lots of pictures, for example.

  • Complicated navigation.

  • Insensitive design—This can include poor color choice, varied designs on each page that don’t relate visually to each other, too many or too few design elements, clutter, outdated material, and more.

  • Text that’s difficult to read.

  • Slow page-load time.

  • Unclear, inaccurate, or wordy headlines.

  • No effective call to action.

  • Ignoring accessibility.

  • Not providing an XML sitemap.

  • Not using analytics (discussed in the next chapter).

  • Poor security—If they sniff out malware or other hacks, they won’t even bother to crawl your site.

  • Not including location in keywords—If your site has a local component such as a store, ensure that you include the location in your keywords. Add a map to your sidebar pointing to your location.

  • Content that merely promotes your pay-per-click products or other ads—Search engines will see this as manipulative and not directly valuable. It’s not nice to fool Mother Google.

  • Ignoring social media—It’s thought that if your site is popular, this can indirectly affect AIO.

  • Tired content—You should update your content frequently, particularly blog posts. And also review your older blog posts to see if you need to revise them to keep them accurate, up-to-date, and using some of your best long-tail keywords.

  • Not optimizing for voice search—Optimize your content for this type of search as well as typed queries. This is another reason to use a conversational tone and long-tail keywords. Also, think in terms of how people would frame a prompt while speaking to AIs.

  • Not taking advantage of Google Search Console.

  • Not choosing a reliable, high-quality hosting service—SiteGround, Bluehost, or DreamHost are recommended. You don’t want your site to suffer from downtime, security vulnerabilities, needless complexity, poor tech support, or slow response time that you can find in abundance if you use a sketchy host. According to estimates, there are 340,000 hosts out there, and many are sketchy.

  • Not including an author bio—Bios helps people relate to you (and builds loyalty). And, if you do it right, you’ll also convey trustworthiness and your expertise.

  • Not using SSL—You don’t want browsers to display Not Secure in the address bar when someone visits your site.

Claude mentioned using knowledge graphs, so I asked GPT to provide an illustration and explanation of that concept. It provided what you see in figure 11.4.

Figure 11.4 A knowledge graph is a visual representation of relationships.

GPT had this to say:

A knowledge graph is a way of storing interconnected information or data that enables the representation of relationships and connections between entities (such as objects, events, situations, or concepts). It’s essentially a graphical representation of a knowledge base, where nodes represent entities and edges represent the relationships between them. This structure allows for a flexible and intuitive way of organizing and querying data, making it particularly useful for semantic searches, data integration, and advanced analytics.

Knowledge graphs are widely used in various applications, including search engines (like Google’s Knowledge Graph), recommendation systems, social networks, and artificial intelligence systems. They help these systems understand the context and relationships between different pieces of information, enabling more accurate and relevant responses to user queries.

The key components of a knowledge graph include:

    •   Entities: The primary objects, concepts, or instances that the graph describes, such as people, places, items, or ideas.

    •   Relationships: The connections or associations between entities, which can be labeled to describe the nature of the connection, such as “is a part of,” “created by,” or “located in.”

    •   Attributes: Information that describes or characterizes entities, providing more detail about them.

The example image shows Person has a relationship HAS_A with Pet.

Person has a relationship WORKS_AT with Company.

Pet is an Animal Type, which BELONGS_TO a Species.

Species LIVES_IN a Habitat.

This diagram showcases how entities (like a person, pet, company, etc.) are interconnected with various relationships, forming a network of knowledge.

Evidently, AI would respect your site more if you included graphs that helped it understand relationships better. Claude also mentioned data visualization. I asked GPT if that was the same as a knowledge graph. And if not, how do they differ? Here’s GPT’s response:

Data visualization and knowledge graphs are related concepts within the realm of AI and data analysis, but they serve different purposes and are not the same thing.

Imagine you have a bunch of Lego blocks. Each block represents a piece of information.

Data Visualization:

Data visualization is like creating a picture or a map out of these Lego blocks. You arrange the blocks in such a way that when you step back, you can easily see a pattern or a shape. This could be anything from a simple bar chart (like stacking blocks to see which pile is tallest) to a complex map showing different data points. The main idea is to help you see the big picture quickly, like how many students prefer pizza over burgers, by just looking at the arrangement of the blocks.

Knowledge Graphs:

Now, imagine instead of just stacking these blocks, you start connecting them with strings based on how they relate to each other. For example, you connect a block representing “you” to another block that represents “your pet dog” with a string labeled “owns.” You might also connect your block to a “school” block with a string labeled “attends.” This network of blocks and strings is like a knowledge graph. It shows not just the blocks (or pieces of information) but also how each block is related to the others through the strings (relationships).

The Difference:

Data Visualization is all about making complex data easy to understand at a glance. It’s like looking at a picture made out of Lego blocks to quickly get what’s going on.

Knowledge Graphs are about showing the relationships between different pieces of information. It’s like seeing how all your Lego blocks are connected with strings, telling a story about how everything is related.

So, while both help us understand information, data visualization is about seeing the big picture quickly, and knowledge graphs are about understanding the relationships and connections between different pieces of information.
