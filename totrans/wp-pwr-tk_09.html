You can monetize—make money with—your website in a variety of ways. It’s your job to ensure that the approach you choose aligns well with your audience, the site’s content, and your personal goals and resources.

In this chapter, we’ll look at the most popular and effective ways to monetize a site. You can make money with most types of sites, one way or another. However, it’s important to understand that even when making money, your primary goal for most sites should remain ensuring that your site satisfies your audience’s expectations with excellent, polished content. And any advertising, sales, or requests for donations should be at least secondary, if not tertiary.

For many kinds of e-commerce, you’ll want to use a plugin. In this chapter, we’ll explore the most popular one, WooCommerce (https://mng.bz/9Yw8).

The topic of e-commerce is vast and varies greatly based on the kind of site you run and the type of sales you intend to pursue. E-commerce could easily fill a book on its own, with each chapter devoted to a different subtopic. Here, we’ll present an overview, with suggestions and guidelines to point you to resources for further detail about your specific use case.

And, of course, these days, the primary essential resource is AI. So, describe in detail your site’s purpose, its audience, and the e-commerce variant you want to employ. Then, submit that to GPT or one of the other AIs. Ask for suggestions about tactics, where you’ll find the strongest competition, how to locate clear documentation, and any other topics you need to address.

10.1 Nine ways to monetize a WordPress site

Here are nine of the most popular ways to monetize a site. Note that some sites use more than one of them:

  • Affiliate marketing—Write reviews or otherwise promote products or services resulting in a commission if your visitor clicks a link that takes them to something to purchase. This kind of marketing comes in two versions: pay-per-sale (PPS) and pay-per-click (PPC). With PPS, you earn money only if there is a sale. This is the most common version and can also be the more rewarding. With PPC, you get paid when someone just clicks on the affiliate link, regardless of whether there is a sale. A variant of PPC is pay-per-lead (PPL), where you’re paid if someone just signs up for a newsletter, fills out a form, joins a website, or other similar results.

  • Advertising—Ads are placed on your site. Google’s AdSense, for example, places ads relevant to your audience. You can choose to place the ads where you wish, or let Google, employing its AI optimization algorithms, choose the places it judges the most effective. It can also decide which kinds of ads would work best and how large they are.

  • Sponsored content—You promote someone else’s products or services. This works best if you have a large site or serve a particular, narrowly focused area of interest (and thus have a well-targeted customer base).

  • Offering your own products or services—Selling digital content, professional services, or physical goods.

  • Drop-shipping—You sell goods, but you don’t need to warehouse your inventory. Your site has a catalog or other e-commerce section, but you avoid the necessity of managing the physical goods. Someone else holds and delivers the goods for you. These suppliers could be wholesalers, manufacturers, or just other retailers. Another advantage is that you don’t risk purchasing inventory that doesn’t move. That allows you to test various products without risk.

  • Subscriptions—You can offer special or premium content, or provide additional features on the site, by charging a subscription fee. If you have unique, specialized, or very high-quality offerings, this approach can result in an excellent income stream.

  • Donations—If your content or services are popular, you can ask for donations. You can ask your audience for direct donations to support your work. GiveWP and Contact Form 7 are popular plugins for requesting donations.

  • Crowdfunding—Similar to donations, you give your customers an option to support your cause, project, or product development. WP Crowdfunding is a good plugin to use for this purpose.

  • Webinars or online courses—Translate your expertise into cash online. Go live in a video conference via a web seminar. You provide online training and share your knowledge with your students. Or create lessons you can offer online, and employ a written discussion area or forum where you can answer questions and students can interact.

  • Licensing content or selling digital assets—If you create original content such as photographs, videos, or digital art, you can license them to others or sell digital assets directly from your website. There is a distinction between licensing and outright selling. With the former, you grant permission for reuse but don’t transfer ownership of the content. And you can set up a recurring fee if you wish. The latter is usually a one-time payment process. And the buyer is likely to get fewer restrictions on usage than with licensing.

10.2 The wrong way to monetize

One great danger with advertising in a website—perhaps the greatest danger—is irritating your audience. The worst offenders are pop-up ads. These intrusive ads cover site content until they are either ready to go away automatically or are closed by the user. Some pop-ups aren’t that easy to close because they don’t display an x for a while, or they hide it in unusual locations (the upper-right corner is where people expect to find the x in Windows). This kind of forced interaction can be exasperating. Some pop-ups even open a new browser tab. Most browsers, however, do feature pop-up blockers.

Another distraction is the use of ads that are animated, flashing, displaying annoyingly bright colors, or otherwise disruptive visuals. And some people are sensitive to this kind of stimulation. So this makes your site less accessible to people facing such challenges.

Many people also resent audio or video that starts playing automatically. Let your customers decide when or if they want to hear or see your ad.

People rarely come to a site to read its ads. So if you crowd your content with too many ads, or you cluster them together, they’re in direct competition with your site’s content. And it’s that content that people are most likely interested in.

Ads not relevant to your site’s topic are also bothersome. They are seen by most of us as an interruption. They likely have nothing to do with your customers’ interests.

Some videos or large, high-resolution image ads can slow down page load time too. You’ll want to test for this because many people around the world have slow internet connections. How to test your page load speeds is explained in the chapter 8, section 8.1.

Continuing to fill out our list of ways to offend via advertising is clickbait. This is when you display a headline or image that turns out to be a trick. The user clicks it to see more, but the more isn’t what the clickbait promised.

We’ll conclude this list of offenders with interstitial ads—a full-page ad that completely blocks the site’s contents. Some such ads are displayed on the home page, and some appear between pages as the visitor moves to view an interior page. Some allow the visitor to close them, while others evaporate only after an interval of their own choosing . . . at last giving the visitor access to the site proper. These kinds of ads certainly command attention because they are as large as can be. If you do choose to use interstitial ads, and you rarely should, take care how you employ them. And give your patient visitors an obvious way to close these ads and get on with their life.

10.3 Choosing the right e-commerce plugin

We website owners usually appreciate additional revenue streams, and e-commerce provides them. If you plan to sell things, you’ll want to choose the best e-commerce plugin for your site. The plugin must be easy to use, efficient, secure, and offer the features necessary for your business.

That said, WooCommerce (by Automattic, WordPress’s parent company) is by far the dominant e-commerce plugin. It’s open source and freemium—so you can start off free, but if you need additional features, you can just upgrade, paying various amounts for the various additional options. You can also add features via the many WooCommerce extensions (more about them shortly).

10.3.1 Why WooCommerce dominates this space

WooCommerce is known to provide an easy-to-use shopping system that makes the purchasing process for clients smooth and effortless. But it’s a truth universally acknowledged that nothing is perfect. So, let’s consider both WooCommerce’s good and not-so-good qualities. We’ll start with the pluses:

  • Extreme flexibility—WooCommerce provides many ways to sell quite a variety of goods and services. It covers all the bases, facilitating every type of e-commerce. It helps you sell actual objects, such as couches or hair products; downloadable books, such as comic books or novels; and external goods, such as affiliate marketing on Amazon, invitations to training courses or webinars, and more. And, if you don’t find what you need in the main WooCommerce system, there are many add-ons available. Go to your Plugins > Add Plugins screen, search for WooCommerce, and you’ll see dozens of popular WooCommerce-related plugins to add specialized functionality.

  • Impressive scalability—Companies from small to huge can operate efficiently within the WooCommerce system. If your sales rocket upward, you need not be concerned that the plugin won’t be able to handle the growth.

  • Countless ways to customize—You can build a store tailored to the kind of goods you sell and the customers you sell to.

  • Excellent protection—Because this is an online platform, your customers expect secure gateways to prevent credential or other data theft. WooCommerce includes safe payment gateways such as Stripe and PayPal. Because the WooCommerce platform is so widely used, hackers regularly target it. To protect your client’s privacy, you can use a backup system that keeps data encrypted. And to further deter hackers, WooCommerce also lets you add a security plugin that periodically checks your website for viruses. You probably already have the Wordfence plugin actively protecting your entire site, and security systems are also active and effective in most hosting services. But redundancy in security is a good thing.

  • WooCommerce Help & Share—Also a private Facebook group, it boasts 41,000 members. This is the advanced group’s little brother. This group is used by all people of all levels of expertise—beginners included.   • WooCommerce Help Page—Go to the WooCommerce > Home screen, and in the upper-right corner, click Help. Choose Get Support or one of the other options.   • The Shopping Cart—Your customers can complete transactions more easily because WooCommerce provides access to excellent point of sale (POS; a website’s checkout system—the shopping cart process) and warehouse management systems. 10.4 Any drawbacks?

Although WooCommerce is quite impressive and a very mature and effective system, it’s not perfect. You should consider a couple of drawbacks that might influence your decision about whether or not to use it:

  • If your website is custom made and isn’t mounted on WordPress, you can’t use WooCommerce. It’s designed to fit into WordPress only. So older, established sites might well be using other systems.

  • If you go beyond the basics, there can be costs. Although WooCommerce can be downloaded and installed for free, you may find you want to upgrade to a paid plan or need to purchase some WooCommerce extensions (similar to plugins) to make your e-commerce efforts successful.

    For instance, you will be responsible for paying for a secure payment gateway if you decide to use something other than PayPal. The majority of WooCommerce extensions require subscriptions.

10.4.1 An alternative to WooCommerce

WooCommerce is considered the best WordPress e-commerce plugin. Because its advantages far outweigh its disadvantages, choosing WooCommerce will probably make things easier overall for both you and your customers.

But if your needs are limited to offering downloadable digital content, and you don’t need all of WooCommerce’s additional functionality, you might take a look at Easy Digital Downloads. This plugin handles sales of digital goods, such as music, e-books, themes, images, PDFs, and so on. True, WooCommerce is also quite capable of handling these kinds of transactions, but it’s a much larger system—and might be more than you require.

Easy Digital Downloads has a very basic interface, which makes it easy to set up. Using the plugin to track and analyze sales is also simple. Go to your Plugins > Add New screen, search for Easy Digital Downloads, and then click the Details button to read all about it.

10.5 Be sure to use Secure Sockets Layer

If you want to drive away prospective customers real fast, the surest way is to force their browser to display a Not Secure warning when they land on your home page. Who’s going to provide their credit card info, or indeed any other personal information, to a site that’s not encrypting communications with its customers? “Not secure” translates in many peoples’ minds as “This way to the dark web.”

The purpose of Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) is to mangle (encrypt) any message from your site to a customer or from the customer to the site. This way, nobody intercepting the transmissions can decrypt that garbled data.

When you add SSL to your site, your URL changes from http to https and lets browsers know SSL is active and thus your site is secure. More recently, an improved system called Transport Layer Security (TLS) has actually replaced SSL. Even though the term SSL is still widely used, in practice, SSL really refers to a TLS system.

SSL was introduced in the mid-1990s by the then-popular browser Netscape. Over time, TLS was introduced and is revised now and then to provide even stronger security. For example, TLS 1.3, the latest version, replaces older, less effective, less secure cryptographic algorithms. It also employs a streamlined handshake process that provides both faster connections and enhanced security.

Some hosting services, such as SiteGround, Bluehost, and DreamHost (excepting DreamHost’s most basic $2.95/month plan), offer free SSL, and the process of installing and verifying it is automatic. But if you’re one of those brave souls who hosts your site yourself on your own hard drive, SSL installation is going to be a rather complex, multistep process.

Note For information about adding SSL to a SiteGround-hosted site, check out https://mng.bz/jp59.

Here are the reasons that SSL is a must for any site, but especially for e-commerce sites:

  • Data security—SSL encrypts the data transmitted between a user’s browser and the website, making it impossible to read the data. This is essential for protecting sensitive data such as personal information that could be used in identity theft, login credentials (passwords), and financial transactions.

  • SEO benefits—Search engines give bonus points in their ranking algorithms to websites using SSL. This means more traffic to your site. (Search engine optimization [SEO] is the topic of chapter 11.)

  • Trust and credibility—Sites with SSL display a padlock or other symbol in the browser’s address bar, telling customers that the connection is secure. Chrome, though, displays a different symbol, which, when clicked, certifies that the site is secure. These symbols can build trust, assuring your customers that the site is legitimate and that their data is protected.

  • Fraud prevention—Certificate authorities issue SSL certificates after they verify the identity of the site owner. This helps to prevent schemes where fraudsters create fake websites to deceive users. These sites often convincingly masquerade as legitimate websites. For example, it’s easy to copy then display an exact replica of a bank’s home page. The goal is to fool people into giving personal data, sending money, or downloading malware or ransomware. (This scheme is called phishing.)

  • Compliance with regulations—Various privacy laws and regulations, such as Europe’s General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), require the protection of personal data as it flows over the internet. SSL helps you comply with these regulations.

10.6 Using a good call to action

A call to action (CTA) is a prompt, button, or some other trigger the user interacts with that’s intended to persuade the customer to do something. Some common results that CTAs trigger are listed here:

  • Downloading a guide or whitepaper—Offer valuable resources or information in exchange for contact details or payment. This is often used in content marketing.

  • Sharing content on social media—Facilitate the sharing of website content on social platforms.

  • Participating in a survey or poll—Engage customers in providing feedback or opinions. This can be a valuable way to gather data on how to improve your site or increase your sales.

Note Chapter 12 focuses on analytics, collecting data about how visitors interact with your site—what path they follow, for example—and therefore how you can make improvements in navigation or other features of your site. In that chapter, you’ll use both AI and plugins. We’ll look shortly at how AI can quickly generate surveys for your audience.

  • Subscribing to a newsletter or mailing list—Encourage customers to stay informed about updates, news, or promotions.

  • Starting a free trial—Invite users to try a service or software for free for a limited time.

  • Registering for a webinar or other event—Prompt users to sign up for online or offline events.

  • Getting a quote or estimate—Offer quotes for services where pricing is customized based on the customer’s needs.

  • Scheduling a demo or consultation—Offer this for services or products that require a demonstration or a personalized consultation.

  • Making a purchase or adding to the checkout cart—Direct customers to buy a product or service.

  • Contacting for more information—Encourage direct communication via phone, email, or a contact form.

  • Accessing exclusive content—Offer access to premium content or members-only areas.

  • Entering a contest or sweepstakes—Invite participation in competitions or giveaways. The goal is to build your brand and sell something later.

The design and wording of the CTA are critical in making it effective. The CTA should be visually striking, so they notice that something needs to be done. It should also clearly state what will happen when the customer clicks the CTA or otherwise activates it. And you should let them see a benefit, or create a sense of urgency.

CTAs can be essential for directing site customers through the purchasing process and winning them over as clients or customers. A CTA is usually an attention-grabbing element, such as a button with a dramatic color or shape. Also, use language that calls for action, like “Buy Now,” “Learn More,” or “Sign Up.” Because CTAs directly affect user engagement and conversion rates, the performance of an e-commerce website can be significantly influenced by the effectiveness of your CTA.

What is a conversion rate?

In e-commerce and digital marketing, a conversion rate is the proportion of website visitors who do what you hope they’ll do. This depends of course on the goals you have in mind. It could be purchasing something, subscribing to a newsletter, downloading a resource, booking an event, or any other key performance indicator (KPI).

KPI is a quantifiable indicator of how well a corporation is accomplishing important goals. In the context of website conversion rate, KPI is a metric to help you see how well your site converts users into customers or leads.

To find the conversion rate as a percentage, divide the total number of visitors by the number of conversions. Then, multiply the result by 100. A website with 2,000 visitors per month and 100 purchases, for instance, would have a conversion rate of (100 ÷ 2000) × 100 = 5%.

Understanding conversion rates lets you gauge how successful a website is in terms of marketing. A low conversion rate means that you should perhaps improve the website’s content, user experience (UX), design, or CTA.

10.6.2 Optimizing conversion

A dazzling, polished website is insufficient to succeed at e-commerce. Your online store must be a conversion machine if you want it to be a big success. Here are some important goals to aim for:

  • Product page perfection—Exceptional, compelling product photos and thorough, accurate product descriptions are essential. Display customer reviews to demonstrate the quality of your offerings.

  • Frictionless checkout—Ensure very easy payment and checkout. To reduce the number of people who just give up and abandon their checkout carts, provide a variety of payment methods, visitor checkout choices, and secure transactions.

  • Mobile-ready tests—In today’s cellphone world, make sure your e-commerce site is optimized to provide a flawless buying experience across all devices. Check your site on a cellphone and tablet. You may need to modify it a bit even though your theme should be doing a good job at this.

10.8 The final checklist

Here is a checklist showing what you should be doing if you want your e-commerce efforts to succeed:

  • Focus—Just as you should focus your entire site on a niche topic, so too should you concentrate on selling a few main products or product categories (at least to begin with). This isn’t true of every type of e-commerce, just most of them.

  • Provide a great UX—Make sure your website is mobile-friendly, visually appealing, and simple to use. You’ll get more extensive visits and more sales.

  • Employ search engine optimization—Once again, make sure you improve your site’s search engine visibility.

  • Install security measures—Put in place robust security procedures to shield your website and client information from online attacks. Always update your theme and plugins whenever updates are available, use secure payment channels, and use an SSL certificate.

  • Offer high-quality content—Post quality blog messages, and add new blog entries on a consistent schedule. Master AI tools to improve the quality of the content in your website. Customers will become loyal, and your SEO will also benefit.

  • Display good product photos and descriptions—Give your products thorough, accurate descriptions along with high-resolution photos. This reduces the possibility of returns while helping customers make the right decisions—choices they won’t regret.

  • Provide exceptional customer service—Offer readily available contact details, FAQs, and live chat assistance (or via an AI chatbot), if possible. Providing solid customer service will result in repeat business and good word of mouth.

  • Give them payment and delivery choices—Offer a variety of payment options and clear delivery guidelines. This can reduce cart abandonment and enhance the UX.

  • Perform analytics and gather feedback—To make ongoing improvements to your website, measure its performance using tools such as Google Analytics, employ AI in several ways, and collect feedback from users (see chapter 12).

  • Perform inventory management—To guarantee a smooth experience and thus satisfied customers, effectively control your stock levels and product availability. Don’t run out of things if you can help it.

  • Ensure legal compliance—Research and adhere to all applicable laws pertaining to your online business, such as those governing cookies, privacy, and e-commerce.

  • Provide multilingual support—Providing your website in several languages might greatly increase your market reach if you’re trying to attract a worldwide audience.

  • Optimize performance—To improve UX and SEO, regularly check and tweak your website’s page-load speeds and other functionality.

  • Use affiliate marketing—Create an affiliate program so influencers and bloggers will send traffic to you and in that way increase sales.

  • Include good return and refund policies—To instill trust in customers’ purchases, establish return and refund policies that are equitable and unambiguous.

  • Upsell and cross-sell—Use techniques to promote more products to customers so you can raise the average order value.

  • Comply with accessibility regulations—Make sure your website is usable by people with impairments. This will increase your clientele and is required by law in many places.
