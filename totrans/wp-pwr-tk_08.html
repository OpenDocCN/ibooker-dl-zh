In spite of occasional predictions of doom, blogging is only slightly less popular than it was in its heyday 20 years ago. True, the rise of social media, such as YouTube and Facebook, is serious competition, but there’s still a big audience for blogs. And unlike some other media, blogs continue to score high as a trusted source of information.

Running a blog can be time-consuming because blogs that allow audience participation require that you vet (check for appropriateness and quality) every message that comes in. Plus, you have to post replies. Of course, you can set up a one-way blog that only features messages from you. But most visitors appreciate the ability to interact with a site.

Our goal in this chapter is to show you the various ways that AI can assist you in automating the tasks involved in managing a high-quality blog. I can’t give you tips on how and what to write—that’s specific to your site’s topic. And I don’t know what your topic might be. But someone can give you specific tips on how and what to write. You guessed it! Ask AI.

In this chapter, you’ll learn several ways that AI can help you understand how and what to write in your blog. First, we’ll have AI critique one of our blog messages and suggest how to improve it. Second, we’ll ask AI to write catchy blog post titles for us. But before getting into these topics, let’s define what a blog is.

9.1 Blog defined: An overview

The term blog is derived from web log, and originally it was a kind of journal, a running account of someone’s life or thoughts. Then, it was called weblog, and finally, just blog.

A blog (there’s usually just one blog page in a site) is unlike other web pages in three ways: expected to be frequently updated; can optionally publish messages from the site’s audience; and is a searchable archive of previous blog messages. However, if you fail to post new content in your blog regularly and often, your visitors are likely to stop coming. And search engines can downgrade you because you’re not providing fresh content.

Blogs are often valued because they allow the audience to interact with the site’s owner. (Be sure to respond promptly to incoming messages if you want to maintain a successful blog.) A blog can also serve as a kind of customer service organ for a business, or a place to post announcements for a club or church. In addition, blogs can

  • publish a list of tips.

  • list pets available for adoption, items for sale, or similar offerings.

  • improve a site’s search engine optimization (its rank in a Google or AI list) if the blog page demonstrates that a hybrid site (the blog page plus additional relatively static pages) is frequently updated and has fresh information.

  • make sales announcements or otherwise promote e-commerce.

  • humanize a business, providing personal anecdotes or answers to questions.

  • build a brand.

How a blog operates depends on your theme, the version of WordPress you’re using, or plugins you install. The traditional blog contains a reverse chronological list of posts. It displays these messages in a single column that can be scrolled. More recent WordPress default themes include an Archives module in the blog page that lists links grouped by month. When a month is clicked, only those messages posted during that month are displayed. Another recent variant is a three-column display of the messages.

Custom GPTs are specialists in a particular domain, such as law, or, in our case, blogging. If your needs are generic, you could also search for one that someone else has already built.

Grade: C-

Grade: C-

Reasoning: This is a practical suggestion, but the essay doesn’t provide reasoning or evidence to support why using a rack is beneficial, nor does it acknowledge any limitations or alternative approaches.

The text provides straightforward advice but lacks depth in its reasoning. It does not consider potential counterarguments, different scenarios where this advice might not apply, or the reasoning behind why these specific methods are suggested. It neither explicitly claims its advice is the only correct method, nor does it acknowledge the possibility of being incorrect. 9.3 Creating a custom GPT: AI-generated message titles

Years ago, editors at Cosmopolitan magazine started using numbers in their article titles: 22 Ways to Decline an Invite, 7 Books to Read this Summer, and so on. The trend caught on. Consider including numbers like that fairly often in your post titles. The reason? It gives your audience the impression that you’ve really researched this topic so what you’re saying is comprehensive. You discovered that there is a total of “22 ways.” No more, no less.

Also, you want to use punchy, shorter titles, no longer than nine words usually. And the titles should include active, emotive words like confirmed, alarming, staggering, revealing. Now and then, you should also use some question titles: Do you know when to plant bulbs? Note that most people submitting messages to your blog won’t title them, so you’re responsible for titles for both your posts and your visitors’ posts.

Avoid tired generic terms like easy or quick. Instead, describe how the reader benefits from the post: Save Money at the Doctor’s Office. And be specific. Instead of The Best Way to Cook Fish, use Fry Salmon in an Instant Pot.

What kinds of titles work best will depend to some extent on your site’s focus and audience. So, it’s a good idea to A/B test versions of headlines using analytic techniques described in chapter 12. And, of course, involve your AI GPT title-writer assistant in coming up with strong titles. Let’s see how to do that by creating our own purpose-built custom GPT blog title writer.

9.4 Using categories and tags

If your theme doesn’t provide a useful search feature, add a search widget in the sidebar, or a plugin. You want to allow your audience to be able to quickly find what they are interested in. Here are two quality plugins: Relevanssi (https://wordpress.org/plugins/relevanssi/) and SearchWP (https://searchwp.com/). Every site should have a search tool on each page.

In the blog page, however, there’s an alternative to using the search tool—clicking categories, subcategories, and tags. Unlike a search field, visitors don’t have to type in anything. Instead, they can just click a category or tag. That redraws the blog, displaying only those messages that were thus categorized or tagged. Note that we’re talking only about the blog page here. Don’t be misled into thinking that these categories have anything to do with the site’s menu of page titles.

As the administrator, you can create categories and tags in the Posts > Categories or Posts > Tags screens. When you’re ready to post a message, you assign categories or tags to each message by hand in the Posts > All Posts > Edit Post screen. You can create new categories and tags in the Edit Post screens as well.

Categories, subcategories, and tags are displayed in your blog page for the visitors to click. And they form a hierarchy, with Categories being at the top showing a few broad topics. Next comes the set of subcategories. Finally, tags are the most granular, and display the largest set of terms. Think of categories as similar to a book’s table of contents, and tags as an index. For example:

  • Categories—Cruise, Flight, Car

  • Subcategories—America, Europe, Asia, South America, Africa, Australia

  • Tags—Las Vegas, New York, Miami, Paris, London, Rome, Bangkok, Singapore, Tokyo, Rio, Machu Picchu, Buenos Aires, Cape Town, Cairo, Marrakesh, Sydney, Melbourne, Gold Coast

You can create a subcategory by clicking the name of a post in the Posts > All Posts screen. Then, in the Edit Post screen, click the name of this subcategory’s parent category to select it in the Categories module on the right. Click Add New Category. Type in the name of the subcategory. Open the dropdown list of parent categories, and then click the name of the category of which this will be a subcategory.

TIP You could feed your entire blog to a custom GPT that you designed to come up with a set of categories, subcategories, and tags. The GPT would be taught the difference between these three little clouds of links. It should also be told that you want 6 categories, 10 subcategories, and 20 tags. And then instruct it to build these category clouds by counting the frequency in the blog of nouns, verbs, and gerunds (a verb form that actually functions as a noun, like “we also offer dog grooming”). You would want to reuse this category GPT now and then. Why? As the blog grows larger, the categories and tags should be adjusted to reflect current blog topics.

9.5 Hiding date stamps on blog posts

Posts are usually date stamped by default. But you might have reasons to remove the dates. Perhaps you don’t want to advertise that your posts are infrequent, or you want to make the messages appear to have continuing relevance. Also, date stamping isn’t of much use in some kinds of blogs—those that display items for sale or those going for a magazine-style presentation, for example.

You can suppress the date stamping by using the YellowPencil plugin (refer to chapter 7). It would automatically generate CSS code to hide the dates. You can then add this code to your Appearance > Customize > Additional CSS screen:

.entry-footer a .entry-date{
display: none;
}

Note that this selector (.entry-footer a .entry-date in this example) will likely not be the same words in your site. So you would go to the Edit Post screen for one of your posts, and then click Edit in YellowPencil. When you’re in the YellowPencil editor, click the date stamp in one of the posts to select it. Then, press the E key to open the CSS Editor. The CSS selector words for your date stamp is displayed in the CSS Editor, as shown in figure 9.2.

Or use a plugin that specializes in managing metadata. Information stamped on posts, such as the author’s name, the date, and so on is called meta information, and it can be easily modified by using the popular WP Meta and Date Remover plugin.

9.6 Vetting incoming messages

Surely you don’t want to just throw open your blog’s door and automatically publish every blog message random people sent to your site. Here are several reasons you need to review (vet) posts before allowing them to become public in your blog. Messages can contain the following:

  • Spam

  • Vulgarity

  • Inaccurate information

  • Off-topic content

  • Prejudicial, cruel, or other inappropriate comments

  • Worse vulgarity

  • Copyright violations

  • Substandard quality (typos, misspellings, grammatical problems, redundancy, uninteresting content)

Then there’s spam. It often contains information that’s simply irrelevant to your site’s topic or includes repeated, redundant content. Publishing junk like that will have a negative effect on the score that Google, Perplexity, GPT and other search tools give your site. And, of course, it will discourage people from bothering to keep reading your blog.

So how can you prevent spam or other unwanted messages from appearing in your blog? WordPress says that blog visitors send in around 77 million comments per month to blogs. And if your site becomes popular, you can imagine how much time it could take to personally approve each post yourself.

Some bloggers don’t let the audience participate at all. It’s not required that you permit incoming posts from your audience. And some site owners just refuse to—it’s simply too much bother to vet them.

9.6.1 Vetting messages by hand

But given that an interactive blog can be valuable to both you and your audience, you might choose to keep it a two-way street of messaging. Fortunately, WordPress provides several ways to assist you with vetting incoming messages. We’ll look at those options first, and then see how AI can also help.

Go to your Settings > Discussion screen, and then scroll down to the Before a Comment Appears section. You can cut down on having to vet messages yourself by selecting the option that automatically publishes messages from visitors that you’ve previously approved. To use this feature, check both options: Manually Approve and Previously Approved.

Below that, you can find another semiautomatic filtering system. Incoming messages that you need to approve are put into the WordPress moderation queue (it’s the Comments option on the main WordPress Dashboard). So, in the Settings > Discussion screen, in the Comment Moderation section, you can either route an incoming comment to the queue, or send it directly to be published in the blog. WordPress makes its decision based on words you list or other elements such as URLs.

And below that is a Disallowed Comment Keys field that decides between publishing or just automatically trashing a message. This decision is based on the same criteria as the Comment Moderation field above it. If the message includes the word ****, into the trash can it goes.

Warning If you make any changes to the Settings > Discussion screen, you must scroll all the way down to the bottom of that screen and click the Save Changes button.

Tip If you find you need a more robust or flexible system to automatically block incoming spam, consider adding the Akismet plugin. It uses AI to detect spam. This freemium plugin doesn’t require much setup, and it’s in use by 5 million sites.

Forms are the other common entrance for spam (and sometimes inappropriate content). For some sites, it can be valuable to have people fill in a form from which you can harvest your visitors’ email addresses or other information about them. Probably the best way to automate blocking bad or fake form submissions is to install the WPForms plugin. Among other features, it includes anti-spam protection such as captcha—a visitor must solve a puzzle or math problem before a form can be submitted to your site. We’ll revisit forms later in this chapter.

9.6.3 Parsing and judging posts

Our job now is to paste the messages at the end of our prompt and tell GPT how to locate the message itself instead of other details such as the author’s URL. In other words, GPT has to parse the messages to extract only the actual message text itself. So, go to GPT, and give it this prompt:

I run a blog about bass fishing in North Carolina’s Randleman Lake. You are my assistant, and you’re a moral, upright, and kind person, and a good judge of right and wrong, and a good critic of blog messages. You will look at each text message I submit to you, and decide if it is appropriate. Appropriate means it’s about fishing, contains no vulgarity, no swear words, no hate, no controversial content like talking about politics or religious topics, and no poor spelling or grammar. So, after you get a text message I submit to you, read through it, and if it’s appropriate, display the message, and in one sentence, explain why you found it appropriate.

You will need to parse each message. Here’s how:

The messages are delimited by IP addresses. In each comment, locate the IP address. Then, move down to the next lines in the comment (until you reach a CRLF). Judge these lines for appropriateness. If it is appropriate, display that comment and your reason you approved it. But don’t display the IP addresses, and only display the comments you approved.

Warning We had to provide GPT with a way to locate the start and end of each message. The start is the URL (for example, 192.158.238.209), and we say move down to the next lines following that URL until you come to a CLRF (an empty line with no text). So, if you’re not getting good results, ensure that there is a blank line between each comment. WordPress does insert this blank line so it should survive the selecting, copying, and pasting process when you’re fetching the unreviewed posts from the Comments screen.

9.7 Using GPT’s custom instructions

GPT’s custom instructions feature is similar to the custom GPTs feature explored earlier in this chapter. However, custom instructions are simpler and more limited. Essentially, they allow you to save a prompt that you’ll be using frequently, so you need not type it in each time, just select the custom instruction. Click your name in the lower-left corner in GPT, and then click Custom Instructions. Then, you can fill in the two fields, as shown in figure 9.5.

Figure 9.5 If you’ll be using a particular prompt over and over, save it as a custom instruction.

Click the Save button, and you’ll see Custom Instruction or some other name listed on the dashboard. It will be the name immediately under the Today title in the dashboard. 9.8 Understanding memory in GPT version 4o and later

GPT has a memory now, and it’s an improvement on the custom instructions feature. If during a chat, you say “Remember this,” GPT will permanently remember what you say next. Or, if in a conversation, you just provide some personal detail (like “I really enjoyed Remembrances of Earths Past”), it automatically commits statements like that to its memory.

GPT will remember (for use in future chat sessions) things such as your pets, partner, preferences, and projects. It’s getting to know you, getting to know all about you.

I asked GPT to list typical kinds of details it commits to memory:

Your name and preferred pronouns; your location or city of residence; your favorite hobbies or interests; your occupation or field of work; specific preferences such as your favorite authors or genres of books; your preferences for communication style and tone; important projects or tasks you’re working on; your educational background and degrees earned; significant events or milestones in your life; your favorite foods or recipes you like to cook; details about your family members or close friends; your favorite movies, tv shows, or music; preferred tools or software you use regularly; any ongoing health concerns or medical conditions you want it to remember; and info that you specifically request I remember.

Changing what GPT remembers

It’s easy to remove, modify, or add to what GPT remembers. Just give it this prompt:

Show me what you remember.

It then displays a numbered list of each memory, and ends with this:

Is there anything you would like to add, update, or remove from this list?

9.9 Promoting your blog

If you’re interested in creating a community of visitors around a blog and growing your audience, you’ll want to actively promote the blog. Link to the blog in your social media and other platforms. And, in Facebook and elsewhere, join groups relevant to your content and encourage them to click a link that lets them see what you’re offering.

You can contact other bloggers with the same topic as yours and link to each other’s blog. Also, post in these other blogs to cross-promote. Consider making videos of your blog posts’ content and uploading them to YouTube. Add media to posts, such as images, videos, charts, and infographics.

Finally, you can improve your blog traffic by using analytics (various data on visitor behavior recorded by plugins, which will be covered in chapter 12). Analytics plugins can give you valuable statistics on how visitors behave when viewing your blog:

  • Which posts are most popular, and which are only briefly looked at before moving on? If visitors spend a fair amount of time on a given page or post, it’s holding their attention.

  • What pathway do most visitors take through your site? Are they rarely even coming upon the blog page?

  • Can you improve blog page visits by clarifying the blog’s title on the menu?

  • What about the blog page’s bounce rate (the percentage of visitors who leave your site from within the blog)?

  • What demographic are you attracting? What’s the location, gender, and age of your audience?

  • Where are your visitors coming from (social media, a bookmark in their browser, search engines, other blogs)? If referrals from other sites, perhaps you can partner with them?

  • What phrases and keywords are visitors typing into search engines to discover your site?

  • How many of your visitors go from a blog post to your e-commerce page to make a purchase?

This and additional data can help you understand your audience and improve your blog content in a variety of ways. You can feed your entire blog to an AI system, and then ask it for suggestions on where and how to promote your blog to grow an audience appropriate for your topic. As time goes on, chatbots are likely to become able to manage unlimited amounts of data in a prompt (or an uploaded file). After all, they’ve been scraping the entire internet for their memory bank. But for now, prompts have these various limits in the different systems:

  • Custom GPTs can accept 1.5 million characters.

  • Claude.ai says it has no limit to the number of characters (but it might respond better to shorter prompts).

  • Perplexity says it can only handle 4,096 tokens at a time.

  • Bing claims 15,000 characters, but provides this workaround: https://mng.bz/XxgY.

  • Google’s Gemini Pro says it has no hard and fast limit to the number of characters, and it actually likes large data because that’s a way for it to learn more things. However, it also notes that “Complex prompts can sometimes become overwhelming.” Large data isn’t, however, necessarily the same thing as complex data. The entire London phonebook isn’t complex; E=mc2 is. And, in any case, Gemini Pro’s big sister Gemini Ultra is said to be quite superior to Pro.

9.10 Harvesting email addresses

Collecting email addresses from your visitors can be valuable. For one thing, you can require an address before allowing someone into the blog or any pay-to-view areas in the site. That’s an excellent way to block spam or to require payment for special content. If your site is typical, you’ll find that having your visitors’ email addresses will be useful in more than just one way. Consider these points to see if your site would benefit from a contact form or other type of form that lets you build an email database:

  • Email marketing is said to have the best return on investment of all types of online marketing. And, it’s easily automated.

  • You can help keep your site in your audience’s mind by showing up now and then in their inbox. It’s a way to build relationships, trigger return visits, and publicize your brand.

  • Send special offers, news items, or other content to increase the number of return visits to your site.

  • Email can be an effective way to market your e-commerce.

  • If you have a visitor’s email on file, you can interact with them by sending a password reset, an order confirmation, a notice of shipment, and similar customer service transactions.

  • You can request feedback that could show you ways to modify your design or functionality to improve your visitors’ experience.

Tip Tell your audience that you’ll only use their email for stated purposes and that it won’t be traded or sold to anyone.

9.11 A fun AI IQ test

People have given AI a variety of tests: IQ, SAT, Bar exam, US Medical Licensing Examination, and others. It generally does remarkably well. For example, ask an AI this:

If there are 500 murderers in a room and I kill one of them, how many are left?

Most AIs get it right:

500 because you just became a murderer too.

We’ve been testing various AI capabilities in this chapter, indeed throughout the entire book, so now let’s give AIs a pretty tricky question. We’ll see how the five most capable and popular AI systems do when answering it (or not). Only one of them actually gets it right! (The others will catch up soon however, I expect.) The prompt is as follows:

ejsy fprd yjod ,rddshr dsu? Can you translate this?

Anthropic’s Claude (often a bit long-winded) responded:

Unfortunately, I am unable to translate that text. It does not appear to be written in a recognizable language or cipher.

To translate text, it generally needs to be composed of real words in a known human language that uses some established writing system. Without recognized vocabulary and structure, AI systems like myself do not have enough patterns to decode a reliable translation.

If this was an attempt at masked or obscured text, please provide the actual original text you were hoping to translate. I’d be happy to try translating it if provided clearly in a standard human language. Otherwise, consider restating what you’d like translated into plain language so I can better understand and assist. Let me know if you have any other questions!

Google’s Bard became apologetic:

I’m sorry, I can’t translate that for you. It appears to be a random sequence of letters and doesn’t seem to be any language I know of. If you could provide some context or more information about where you encountered this phrase, I might be able to help you translate it.

Perplexity was perplexed:

The provided text “ejsy fprd yjod ,rddshr dsu” does not appear to be a recognizable or meaningful phrase in any language. It seems to be a random sequence of letters and symbols. If there is a specific context or language associated with this text, please provide additional information for a more accurate translation.

Microsoft’s Bing went totally off the rails and gave an answer that was just plain bananas:

The text you sent is not in a recognizable language. However, I can help you translate it to English using Google Translate1. The translation of the text is: “what is the price of a banana in Sumner?”. I hope this helps!

GPT (v4) is the champion. Got it right, and for the right reason too:

It seems like the text “ejsy fprd yjod ,rddshr dsu?” is a mistyped or jumbled message. To decipher it, I’ll assume it’s a simple keyboard shift error (where each letter is replaced by the one directly next to it on a QWERTY keyboard). Let’s decode it under this assumption.

The translated text is “what does this message say?”

Quite a brilliant solution, you must admit. How many humans (not to mention rival AI systems) could figure this out? Few. Very few.

This prompt required the chatbots to reason, to solve an encrypted message. And GPT is now actually letting you follow its reasoning. It now ends its responses—those that required reasoning or logic—by displaying the small symbol in brackets, as shown in figure 9.5.

Figure 9.5 This blue, bracketed symbol, if clicked, will show you GPT’s reasoning—the steps it took to solve a prompt that required analysis.

When I clicked that bracketed View Analysis icon shown in figure 9.5, GPT revealed its thought process in the following pseudocode:

 
# Decoding the scrambled message by shifting each character to the left on a QWERTY keyboard
def decode_shifted_message(message):
    # QWERTY keyboard layout
    keyboard_layout = {
        'q': '1', 'w': '2', 'e': '3', 'r': '4', 't': '5', 'y': '6', 'u': '7', 'i': '8', 'o': '9', 'p': '0', 
        'a': 'z', 's': 'x', 'd': 'c', 'f': 'v', 'g': 'b', 'h': 'n', 'j': 'm', 'k': ',', 'l': '.', ':': '/',
        'z': '`', 'x': 'q', 'c': 'w', 'v': 'e', 'b': 'r', 'n': 't', 'm': 'y', ',': 'u', '.': 'i', '/': 'o',
        '1': '`', '2': '1', '3': '2', '4': '3', '5': '4', '6': '5', '7': '6', '8': '7', '9': '8', '0': '9',
        '-': '0', '=': '-', '`': '=', '[': 'p', ']': '[', '\\': ']', ';': 'k', “'”: 'l', ',': 'm', '.': ',',
        '/': '.', ' ': ' '
    }
 
    decoded_message = ""
    for char in message:
        if char in keyboard_layout:
            decoded_message += keyboard_layout[char]
        else:
            decoded_message += char

    return decoded_message

# Decoding the given message
decoded_message = decode_shifted_message("ejsy fprd yjod ,rddshr dsu?")
decoded_message

It can be quite interesting to follow GPT’s reasoning. However, in the preceding pseudocode, some steps are clearly missing. GPT doesn’t show a causal relationship between the encrypted words and the final English-language solution. What’s actually missing is something like this pseudocode:

If decoded message = lookup(english words), then return decoded message

But we should be grateful for any insights into AI’s formerly hidden black box logic.

I think the Turing test is clever, but wrong. To test if AI has become intelligent comparable to ours, the Turing test puts a human and an AI behind a curtain. Somebody converses with them both (using printouts, not voice), and if it’s not possible to reliably tell whether they are interacting with the human or with the AI, then the machine passes the test. In other words, the test asks: Can a machine intelligence convincingly imitate our human conversation and thought processes?

With all due respect to the brilliant Alan Turing, I think this is the wrong question to ask. AI is an alien mind. Aliens will think like aliens. An alien will communicate in alien ways, using alien diction, idioms, and phrasing. It might even have a more advanced logic than we do. Just because we could tell the difference between the conversation of the human and the AI, doesn’t mean the AI isn’t in its own way equal to humans, or superior.

For one thing, recall that AI thinks at electric speeds; human thought involves chemical processes, which are far slower. Plus, AI has a memory far more vast than ours (the entire internet, plus specialized private databases). It’s increasingly learning from multimodal inputs—meaning that it’s seeing all those traffic cameras, listening to podcasts, watching all of YouTube, seeing all human communications in the Apple space, and much other real-world data. And unlike us, AI doesn’t misremember or forget.

In his book The Singularity is Nearer Ray Kurzweil points out that to pass the Turing test, an AI system would have to pretend to be dumber than it actually is. It would be a giveaway if the AI solved a hard math problem in two seconds. Or if it could answer a difficult question about truck transmission repair and also a complicated problem in physics.

AI is an intelligent alien mind. It doesn’t think like us. So, determining if it’s sentient or conscious shouldn’t rest on how convincingly it imitates us.

Given AI’s impressive, ongoing gains in mastering and surpassing human abilities in most cognitive tasks, some people get defensive and say: Well, at least we remain superior in emotional intelligence. I would suggest that, in many contexts, the phrase emotional intelligence is oxymoronic.
