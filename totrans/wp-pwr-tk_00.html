1.1 Why WordPress wins

Most other website-building systems have fallen by the wayside over the years. WordPress, however, has grown to dominate the internet, and now enjoys 65% of the market share. The closest runner-up is Joomla, with only 4.6%. Until WordPress came along, building a website required you to struggle with the three terrible “T’s”—time, technicality, and tedium. Most people simply couldn’t do it! WordPress made its debut in 2003, and the result was that constructing websites quickly became far simpler and far more efficient. And since 2011, WordPress usage has increased, on average, 12% each year.

WordPress is a major improvement over traditional site-builders like DreamWeaver and Drupal. For one thing, WordPress eliminates the need to write programming code. As a result, pretty much everyone can now build impressive sites for themselves—whether the site’s purpose is professional, commercial, educational, personal, or business. And as you’ll see, a great site can be built in weeks, not months.

Let’s take a look at the primary reasons that WordPress is so superior to the alternatives:

  • Easy to install—A simple, one-click installation tool available in most WordPress hosting services is all that’s needed to mount a new, empty WordPress site on your hosting account. No technical knowledge is required.

  • No computer programming on your part—WordPress is code-free (unless you want to code). People with no technical background find they don’t need to know how to do programming. WordPress creates programming code behind the scenes for you. You work visually by moving a slider, dragging and dropping, clicking to choose from a list of options, and so on. You produce your site pages in a highly efficient system that is graphical, not programmatic.

    You create and modify site content without needing to know how to program HTML, PHP, JavaScript, or CSS code. You adjust page layout, response to a button or link click, text qualities, and other site elements with your mouse, not mind-numbing keyboard coding. Put simply, the user interface is effective, friendly, and intuitive.

  • A large collection of professionally designed themes—If you prefer, you can leave your site design up to expert designers. You can choose from more than 30,000 free or premium themes. A WordPress theme governs how a site looks—the color scheme, layout, typefaces, and other elements of style. You can, however, customize and modify these default visual elements to make your site unique. And, you can make it easy for nontechnical people to master quickly.

  • Customizable—WordPress is highly customizable, making it simple for users to modify their website’s design and functionality. If you’re so inclined, you can add your own programming code, but it’s not required.

  • A huge selection of plugins—You can find more than 60,000 plugins in the official WordPress directory alone! They add functionality to a site, such as an animated image gallery, a shopping cart checkout system, a set of thousands of fonts, a backup system, connections to social media, and many more features. You install these plugins with a simple mouse click.

  • Built-in media management—WordPress includes a built-in Media Library that makes it easy to upload, organize, modify, and insert images and other media into your posts and pages.

  • Responsive design—Most popular WordPress themes are designed to be responsive, which means they automatically adjust your content to the size of the screen it’s viewed on, making it easy to create mobile-friendly websites even on a large computer monitor. However, before the site is made public, you should test it on various screen sizes: cellphone, tablet, and computer monitor. This way, you can tweak things if need be.

  • SEO-friendly—WordPress is built with search engine optimization (SEO) in mind, making it possible for you to modify your site to achieve a higher rank when people search for your site’s topic.

  • Efficient page-building—You’ll find many tools that streamline constructing a successful site in a short time.

Now that we’ve set out why WordPress is an excellent choice in the vast majority of website-building contexts, let’s move on to the other major topic of this book—AI and how it can help take your work in WordPress to the next level.

1.2.3 You move from creator to curator

Notice that when you employ AI for tasks like these, you move up to a higher position in the organization. AI is your assistant. So that makes you, by definition, its boss. You don’t have to do research and produce rough drafts. AI does that. You become a curator rather than a creator.

AI presents you with several titles, essays, illustrations, or whatever, and you then pick the best one, ask AI to try again, or tell AI to make some specific changes to the one you like most. You’re not a graphic artist; you’re the head of the art department.

If AI writes a draft of an article you assigned to it, then you become the editor, judging and polishing the writing. Or, you send it back for revision or fact-checking. But ultimately, you’re a senior editor with a junior assistant. Or when brainstorming, you pick the best of several suggestions. You originate a project, providing concepts, instructions, and goals rather than preparing preliminary sketches, researching, or doing first drafts. You oversee apprentices who do the legwork and the prep work and bring you samples to pick from. In other words, you don’t originate content. You propose, evaluate, and critique it.

1.3 How to use artificial intelligence

Let’s explore the many ways you can use AI, and focus first on learning to communicate with machine intelligence. This is known as prompting, and it’s a form of high-level programming. You type in a request or, in some instances, converse verbally with the AI.

Traditionally, computer programming demanded a considerable investment of time to learn a computer language. These languages were usually obscure, truly deserving the term coding. It was as if you were using a cryptic, semi-symbolic dialect. Errors (“bugs”) were common and could be challenging to solve.

Now, all that’s changed. The distance between humans and machines is shrinking. You communicate with AI in your natural language, not in a computer language. So, communication is much easier. But still, much like directing an assistant, you do want to be specific, as you’ll soon see.

1.5 Mastering AI prompting

The foundation of most good prompts includes four elements: role, job, context, and format. And for each one, the more specific you are, the better. In this section we’ll explore these four elements in a bit more depth, consider how to use them together, and look at a few other concepts behind mastering the art of AI prompting.

1.5.1 Role

AIs are quite skilled at simulating things including types of people. You’ll usually get a superior response if you tell an AI its personality or expertise or both:

You are an expert at cooking Northern Italian dishes, specifically the dishes of Bologna.

Right from the start, give AI a role, like lawyer or art critic. Begin a prompt with variations on “you are a skilled mechanic” or “act as a master cake decorator.” AI sometimes demurs, saying that it’s not a lawyer or that, as an AI, it doesn’t have personal opinions. But often it follows that reluctance with a good answer anyway. (You can also slightly rephrase your prompt to get it to answer, if need be.)

Giving AI a role seems to affect the result in two ways. First, the response can be more trustworthy because it’s drawn from experts. And second, the diction and idioms fit the role you gave it. For example, “you are a truck driver” can result in “go double nickel here and you’ll get a bumper sticker,” which, when translated, means “do 55 and you’ll get tailgated.”

You can even tell AI that it’s a particular character or person:

Act as Mickey Mouse.

You are Elvis.

If you want a response of superior precision, where the sentences are as clear as a country stream, just tell AI that it’s Cardinal Newman or Hemingway.

You also have the option to describe the tone of voice—conversational, formal, legalistic, whatever. (This might not be necessary if the role you gave it determines the writing style.) A variation on this is specifying your intended audience—children, art students, soldiers, a congregation. A good way to get a simpler, clearer description of a complex topic is to prompt like this:

Explain quantum computing to a 10-year-old. And use metaphors or analogies. Also display diagrams or pictures to illustrate the concept.

1.5.2 Job

Clearly describe the task you’re asking the AI to complete. Specificity especially helps here, so add some modifiers:

Provide a popular recipe for vegetarian meatballs that could be found in a great restaurant in Bologna.

You can even request that AI ask you questions:

Give me step-by-step instructions to create a casserole. Ask me the necessary questions about it, then after I answer them, provide the recipe.

Type of Protein, Starch Base (Pasta, rice, or potato?), Vegetables, Sauce, Any Toppings, Spice Level, and Dietary Restrictions.

Specify multiple elements:

Will I likely need to replace the suspension of my 2005 Ford 150 XLT? The truck is in fair condition, has 220,000 miles, and is driven in Pittsburgh with lots of potholes.

The more detail you provide, the closer the result will be to your objective. But you can always ask follow-up questions to maneuver AI closer to your goal as well.

TIP If AI provides a result that is too general, inappropriate, or simply wrong—tell it so and ask for another response. You can either pose the same question in a different way, ask it to elaborate, give it some additional details, or request that it rethink (or fact-check) its response. Try telling it to rewrite the answer as a step-by-step list or a to-do list. Remember, one way AI differs from traditional search engines is that you’re in a conversation with it, and you can guide it to your goals by dialoguing with the AI. And do be direct, even blunt, for best results.

AIs tend to react to our corrections by apologizing and thanking us for the information. (What they really might be thinking could be another story entirely. AIs have been caught in various kinds of deliberate deception.) They store such corrections in their memory to avoid making the same mistake again. Don’t be shy. AI (it’s claimed anyway) doesn’t have feelings to hurt, so if it provides a poor answer, go ahead and type one of the following:

Give me some evidence that supports your answer.

Why do you say that?

That’s too long; make your answer about half as long.

That’s just wrong. Try again.

Some experts have said that you can get better results if you’re forceful in your prompting, rather than polite. Instead of “Please” say “You have to.” Even use a warning, like this:

If you change the tone of this essay, you will be reprimanded.

(Try this approach and see if it makes a difference.) Remember, at this stage in our relationship with AI, we’re still in charge.

1.5.3 Context

Describe the situation or the background related to the task. This can be information about the purpose, the intended audience, examples, or any other contextual details:

This will be used as a demo recipe for professional chefs wanting to master classic Bolognese cooking. So you need not include obvious beginner instructions such as “be gentle when forming the balls or they’ll toughen.” This audience knows things like that already.

Or, give it a time or place context:

13th century architecture, Greek geography, Mexican history.

In addition, some AI systems seem to respond better if you repeat a key term for emphasis:

Create an excellent, compelling, painterly oil painting of a Peruvian mountain village. Show several villagers. It is a quiet afternoon, and the villagers are wearing traditional Peruvian villager clothing.

Sounds redundant, but see if repeating your main objective or primary focus like that helps AI deliver the goods better. Here, we wanted to emphasize villagers.

1.5.4 Format

Do you want a list? Song lyrics? A 5,000-word short story? A five-paragraph overview? Tell AI what you want with specific instructions like this:

Start with estimated total cooking time and calorie count. Then list the ingredients. Then follow with numbered step-by-step instructions.

Specify the length and genre you’re after:

A limerick, a blog post, comma-separated values, listed in order of popularity, summarized in one paragraph

Try to avoid negative prompts. You should use terms that describe the idea, not its opposite. In other words, write easy rather than not hard.

If you’re getting florid and gassy responses from an AI, use these terms often in your prompt:

Clarity, concision, detail. Don’t use any “poetic” or flowery diction.

In addition, you can ask AI to reformat text you submit to it. For example, give it a paragraph and ask that it display the sentences as an alphabetized list. Or paste a long article and ask it to summarize the content in one paragraph.

Optionally, provide a specific example of what you’re looking for. Give it a poem you like, and tell it to use that poetic style. Or use prompts like these:

Write a multi-paragraph essay in the style of this excerpt. [put your sample of the writing style here. It can be your writing, or a famous writer]

or

Revise each paragraph in this essay. But make sure it retains its conversational style. I want you to only focus on ensuring that the diction and grammar are improved. Do not change the tone and style, or you will be scolded.

TIP Currently, AI isn’t great at accurate word count because it works with tokens. These are units of text, but not necessarily words. Tokens don’t map 1:1 to words. For example, a punctuation mark is a token. The term ice cream is only one token. So, if there’s a lot of punctuation, AI responds with fewer words than you requested, or if there are several multi-word tokens, you’ll get more words. Claude, when asked, defends its refusal to give you a response with the exact word count you asked for because “Metrics like coherence, relevance, factual accuracy are prioritized instead.” In other words, it prefers to offer quality rather than quantity. Fair enough. By the way, how many tokens do you think AI systems are being trained on? It’s difficult to get this data, but several sources say that Meta’s largest LlaMA model was trained on 1.4 trillion. That benchmark has undoubtedly grown higher by the time you read this book.

1.5.5 Putting the elements of prompting together

Here’s an example prompt that illustrates how to set up a dialogue and how to use the four elements of good prompting: role, job, context, and format:

You are an experienced professional site designer and know how to give websites really effective, creative site titles. Please help me brainstorm titles for my new website. The audience for this site is bass fishermen who want to learn how best to catch wide-mouth bass in Randleman Lake in NC. The purpose of the site is to teach bass fishing techniques specific to this lake and also to sell my handcrafted lures. Ask me questions, but after each question, wait for my answer. Make the tone of your suggested titles playful and inventive. But at the same time, these titles must convey the site’s purpose and include the terms “Bass Fishing” and “Randleman Lake.”

In that example, I’m asking GPT to pose a series of questions to me, like a real brainstorming session. This process will narrow the focus and can produce specific and useful results. Here’s the dialogue I had with GPT after submitting that prompt:

Okay, let me start by asking a few questions to get a better understanding of the vibe you’re going for with this website:

What kind of personality or brand image do you want to convey? Serious and technical? Fun and lighthearted? Something in between?

Lighthearted, but accurate and informative nonetheless

Here are some playful yet informative title ideas for your bass fishing website focused on Randleman Lake and selling handcrafted lures:

“Hooked on Randleman: A Bass Angler’s Playground”

“Randleman Lake Bass Fishing: From Dock to Dinner”

“Randleman Lunkers: Lures and Lessons for Landing Largemouths”

“Bass Blitz at Randleman: Mastering the Lake’s Finned Fighters”

“Randleman’s Raging Reds: Taming the Lake’s Feisty Bass”

Let me know if you need any other creative title ideas that balance fun and function!

Although GPT’s idea of creative seems to lean heavily on alliteration, it did give me some ideas worth considering. I’d probably follow up by requesting that it limit itself to only two alliterated words per title though.

My choice would be “Randleman Lake Bass Fishing: From Dock to Dinner.” You could ask AI for some variations on your personal favorite, or ask which is its favorite.

1.5.6 Asking the right questions

The questions you ask an AI system will determine the quality and usefulness of what AI produces. The main thing to remember is that a solid prompt is specific. This is an example of a prompt that’s not specific enough:

Write about a businesswoman who owns a bakery.

You can get a better, more useful response if your prompt includes more details, like this:

You are a prize-winning human-interest writer for a popular magazine. Write a 1500-word character sketch of a 35-year-old woman who is a successful bakery owner and who has useful advice about maintaining a good work-life balance. Include details about her appearance, personality, daily routine, and relationships with others.

Google vs. AI

Most AIs seem to prefer prompts that are complete sentences, or at least descriptive phrases, as if you were in conversation with a human. This is unlike how most people use Google, where they usually just type in a few keywords.

To oversimplify a bit: AI systems can have a conversation with you and clearly understand the context and your preferences (if you remember to provide them). Google just matches keywords without nuanced comprehension. Google gives you a list of links you can visit where your keywords match its database, so you can search for answers; AI gives you actual answers. However, Google and Edge are rapidly infusing AI into their search engines, so you can expect improvements in those systems as well.

1.5.7 How many words?

There’s no consensus on prompt length. Google says 21 words is just about right when prompting its Gemini AI system. Too many words and Gemini can get confused; too few and its response will probably not address your specific needs. Other AI systems don’t specify an ideal word count. They say the number of words should be determined by the task at hand.

Some experienced prompt engineers recommend short prompts on the theory that too much information confuses the AI, and it forgets or overlooks things nearer the end of a long prompt. Other engineers claim that you need to provide a lengthy, detailed prompt. Yet others say to create a long description of your request but break it up into several consecutive prompts. I’m confident that as AIs improve, you’ll soon be able to ignore this metric. They’ll be able to handle prompts of any length just fine.

1.5.8 AI itself can improve your prompts

Here’s another technique. You ask AI to quiz you a bit about your initial prompt, enhancing it by making it more specific:

You’re an expert at helping people get rid of clutter. Please tell me how I can deal with my house which is a bit too full of things I don’t really use. Before answering, ask me four questions that will make your advice about decluttering better, more specific to my particular situation. Pause after each question to let me answer it. Then, when finished, display a new, improved prompt that refines my original prompt. Then answer that new prompt.

A related tactic is to ask one AI to prompt another:

GPT, you are a well-trained artist and skilled at prompting Midjourney to get the best results. Provide me with a good prompt that will produce a very photorealistic, detailed, picture of a blonde model in her twenties showing off a silk cocktail dress.

GPT got creative, adding details that made the prompt much more specific:

A photorealistic image of a blonde model in her twenties, with striking blue eyes and flawless skin, standing confidently. She is wearing a luxurious, elegant silk cocktail dress that shimmers in the light, perfectly fitted to highlight her figure. The dress is a deep sapphire blue, with a delicate V-neckline and a slightly flared skirt that falls just above the knees. The setting is a high-end fashion show runway with soft, ambient lighting that enhances the model’s features and the texture of the silk dress. The background is blurred to keep the focus on the model and her attire. Every detail, from the subtle stitching on the dress to the soft waves in her hair, is captured with high definition and realism.

Make whatever adjustments you wish. However, this expanded prompt will probably give you some ideas you wouldn’t have thought of.

Finally, the Discord version of Midjourney has a Describe option. You type /describe, upload an image or two, and then ask it to describe (create a prompt) based on the uploaded image(s). It responds with four prompts for you to select from. Or you can click the Imagine All button and get a total of 16 variations or more.

1.5.9 Inserting your role

One way to get AI to give you more than just a summary of an article or other block of text is to tell it what your job or role is. AI will respond with actionable ideas and steps you can take to accomplish something, rather than just a mere summary:

Below I’ve pasted a brochure on visiting New York City. What information in this article will be useful to me when I take my children to learn about the art you can see in this city?

GPT ignored all the info in that tourist brochure about The Battery, the statue, and such and just listed the major museums, describing in each case why they are worth seeing. It also creatively suggested the Natural History Museum because kids love it and there is some art. That last suggestion illustrates how GPT is now no longer a strictly literal answer bot but understands the overall context of a prompt, in this case, the children.

1.5.10 Learning the Chain of Thought method

A study by DeepMind revealed that one good way to start a prompt is with this curious phrase:

Take a deep breath and work on this problem step-by-step.

DeepMind reported that when they used these words, Google’s PaLM 2 large language model’s (LLM) score on some elementary school math problems improved from 34% to 80.2%. Tests like this demonstrate the importance of learning to use good prompting techniques.

The idea of asking AI to step logically through its response is called tree of thought (ToT, aka chain of thought [CoT] and thought decomposition). We did this earlier in the chapter when asking AI to ask us questions, pause, and wait for our answers. Another word that describes ToT is reasoning.

For some types of queries, ToT results in measurably superior answers (https://mng.bz/1aeq). You can and usually should combine several prompt best practices into a single prompt. This next prompt uses both role assignment and ToT:

As an expert website designer, list the steps in your thought process when coming up with a new website for recipes for the Instant Pot. Specify the site’s overall organization, its theme, and how you plan to make it popular. After each step, pause before you go any further, and ask me if I have any suggestions. Wait until I respond.

You can use a similar approach when you’re drilling down from a more general overview to a specific recommendation. Here’s an example that combines several prompt techniques plus a ToT drill-down:

As an expert website designer, list the steps in your thought process when discovering the best resources when you have a question about how to do something in WordPress. First, find five good types of resources (for example, WordPress forums, hosting sites’ tech support, or a friend of yours). Then, the second step is to evaluate these resources and decide on the two most useful. Explain your reasoning. Finally, choose the best resource of all, and provide an example of how to best use it. After each of these steps, pause before you go any further, and ask me if I have any suggestions. Wait until I respond.

Not all AI systems will follow your request to pause and wait for your reaction after each step. Currently, GPT 4o, Copilot, and Claude will, but Perplexity won’t. However, Perplexity does display the steps it takes when responding to any prompt. So, you do see its CoT, and you can request elaboration.

1.5.11 GPT o1 does chain of thought on its own

In September 2024, GPT introduced a new AI, version o1, that specializes in math, analytical reasoning, programming, and logical deduction. Although slightly slower than previous GPTs, it’s superior in some ways. I asked GPT o1 the following:

Describe how you, GPT o1, use CoT reasoning.

GPT o1 replied:

Chain-of-Thought reasoning allows the model to generate intermediate reasoning steps when solving complex problems or answering questions. Instead of producing a direct answer, the model internally works through the problem step-by-step, which helps in arriving at more accurate and coherent responses.

How GPT-4 o1-preview Uses Chain-of-Thought Reasoning

Internal Reasoning Process:

The model generates hidden reasoning steps that are not always displayed to the user.

These steps involve breaking down the problem into smaller parts, analyzing each component, and synthesizing the information to form a final answer.

Improved Context Understanding:

By considering the chain of thoughts, the model maintains better context over long conversations or complex queries.

This helps in reducing errors that stem from misunderstanding or overlooking critical details.

Enhanced Problem-Solving Abilities:

The model can handle multistep reasoning tasks more effectively, such as mathematical calculations, logical deductions, and analytical reasoning.

It can explain the reasoning process when required, aiding in educational contexts where understanding the solution path is as important as the answer.

Interactive Explanations:

Users can prompt the model to show its chain of thought explicitly.

This transparency allows users to follow the model’s reasoning, verify correctness, and gain insights into how the model approaches problems.

Dynamic Reasoning Paths:

The model can adapt its reasoning based on new information or corrections provided during the conversation.

It supports iterative refinement, enabling it to adjust its answers as the dialogue progresses.

Benefits of Chain-of-Thought Reasoning in GPT-4 o1-preview

Accuracy: By working through problems step-by-step, the model reduces the likelihood of errors.

Transparency: Users can see how the model arrived at an answer, which builds trust and facilitates learning.

Versatility: The model performs better across a wide range of tasks, from simple queries to complex problem-solving.

User Engagement: Interactive explanations keep users engaged and can enhance the learning experience.

Just when we thought CoT required a human prompt, AI takes over that job too. Prompting GPT o1 might require a different approach now. You might want to ask GPT what kind of prompt works best, now that GPT is capable of its own CoT process and can create intermediate steps when reasoning.

1.5.13 Can AI auto-prompt itself?

It was bound to happen. Lately people have been studying prompt engineering (like we did in the previous sections of this chapter). That’s a useful skill for now, but perhaps not for much longer.

Let’s face facts: AI will soon write prompts superior to those we can think up. First, it knows lots of things that we don’t know. Its memory is the size of the internet and contains most recorded human knowledge. (That’s why it’s already far better, for example, than most doctors at things such as reading X-rays and making diagnoses.) And second, AI knows itself better than we do. So, it understands how to write prompts that can get the best results.

So how does auto-prompting work? Let’s try it out.

You type in a prompt idea, and Claude will flesh it out for you into an improved, well-engineered prompt. Claude creates good prompts and then submits the prompt to itself so you can test it right then.

To try it out, go to https://console.anthropic.com/. Sign up or sign in. Then, click the Generate a Prompt button.

You can provide short prompts (for example, “Write an email”), and then use Claude’s Edit feature to redo or modify the long prompt it generates if need be.

I wanted AI to edit my writing to give me some inspiration about simplifying my paragraphs and making them beautiful. I wouldn’t copy and paste AI’s revision. I’d just get some good suggestions, particularly in matters of diction. So, I asked Claude this:

1.6 A 13-step program: Creating a great website with WordPress

People creating and maintaining WordPress sites face 13 major tasks. This book’s chapters cover them in the order they are usually encountered while building a website. So let’s take a look at the typical steps people take while constructing a good site. The diagram in figure 1.4 illustrates the site-building steps.

Figure 1.4 An overview of the process of creating a new WordPress website (Diagram courtesy of Custom GPT “Diagrams: Show me”)

After this big picture view of the process, let’s then run through each of these steps in a little more detail.

1.6.1 Choosing the best hosting service

Before actually working on your site, you need to take several important steps. Locate and sign up with the best possible hosting service. You’ll work on WordPress in the cloud, and your site will be stored and secured on your host’s servers. WordPress itself is free to use as it’s open source.

The best hosts are DreamHost, Bluehost, and SiteGround. These are the three recommended by WordPress itself. Even experienced site developers run into problems, and a host with excellent tech support—fast and accurate—is, for most of us, essential.

Although difficult, it’s possible to host a site on your own hard drive. However, you’ll want to use the utilities, security, speedy page load time, tech support, and many other features supplied by a hosting service. They can be quite reasonable, and they make building, maintaining, and protecting your site much easier.

1.6.2 Preplanning

A website should be organic—it should be well-focused and coherent. If you decide to wing it, to improvise as you go along, it’s all too easy to randomly tack things on. The result can be a patchwork, a disjointed collection of features and designs that don’t really work together to result in a clear overall purpose and context. Transitions between the pages can seem jarring. Site navigation can be inefficient and design can look motley. In this book, we’ll look at several ways to ensure cohesion and consistency right from the beginning of site construction.

It seems illogical, but a site with a narrowly focused topic almost always attracts and retains a larger audience. People planning their site topic often mistakenly think that a lot more visitors would be attracted to a general topic like fishing, for example. But unless you’re a celebrity fisherman, a topic that generic can easily sink your online efforts into oblivion. The reason is simple, if counterintuitive: competition. Search Google for “fishing,” and you get more than 3 billion results. Google “fly fishing in the Oconaluftee river” reduces your competition to zero or only a few.

The only unfocused websites that tend to succeed are about celebrities, who thus have a built-in audience already. And that audience is interested in a whole variety of topics relating to the celebrity’s travels and other activities.

1.6.3 Setting up the site structure

Most people are very surprised when they find out that you can, in a few hours, set up all the pages and subpages on your site! It’s only a matter of clicking an Add New button and typing in the page title to fill the site with empty placeholder pages. Later on, you’ll fill them with text and media content. But creating your overall site structure is surprisingly fast and simple.

1.6.4 Writing in a conversational style

Most website content is written in a relaxed, inviting style. Unless the content is academic, legal, or for some other audience that expects formal writing, you should aim for a conversational tone. Write as if you were talking with a friend. Chapter 4 shows you how to use AI to rewrite your text content in various styles.

1.6.5 Choosing the right plugins

Plugins add functionality to a WordPress site. Do you need a button users can click to translate your site into another language? Would you like to display your artwork in an animated slideshow? Does your site’s blog need to be protected from spam? There are about 60,000 plugins, which is both good and bad. You’ve got lots of options, but you want to learn how to pick the best ones.

The good part is you can find quite a few excellent plugins to choose from, and many are even free. The bad part is that no plugin developer has time to test their plugin for compatibility with the other 59,000. If you notice some odd behaviors (bugs), take a look at your plugins first. Plugin side effects are the single most common cause of bugs in WordPress. That said, most plugins, especially the very popular ones, play well together.

DEFINITION A plugin is a software component that adds some specific functionality to a site. Examples include an events calendar, a contact form, social media integration, speeding up page loads, and a spam blocker, among others. Plugins are similar to cellphone apps—they are free or paid, can add functionality, and can be easily installed or removed at will.

DEFINITION A side effect is essentially a bug—some weird behavior like something that should be visible on a page but isn’t. Or, you see an error message. Bugs can cause crashes, freezes, incorrect display of information, or other unexpected behaviors. If you have a bug in WordPress, the first step in debugging is to temporarily deactivate all of your plugins (it’s easy to do) and then reactivate them one by one. When the bug reappears, the plugin you just reactivated is the culprit. Uninstall it, and find an alternative plugin. With tens of thousands of available plugins, you won’t have any problem finding a good substitute. Google “best WordPress contact form plugins,” or ask an AI. Given the wide choice of quality plugins available, there’s little reason to waste time trying to figure out why one of yours is causing problems.

In this book, we’ll explore how to choose reliable plugins that are unlikely to be abandoned by their creator, play well with others, and are kept up-to-date so they don’t introduce security vulnerabilities. One good strategy is to choose popular plugins that are marketed in two tiers—a free and a paid version. These freemium plugins provide an income stream for their developers and are thus probably going to be kept current.

1.6.6 Site design basics

Where plugins add features to a site, themes add professional design. Many design elements won’t be consciously noticed by most of your visitors. Colors, layout, typefaces, and so on are all below most people’s radar. But that doesn’t mean that people aren’t affected by design—quite the opposite!

AI can provide you with surprisingly useful critiques and suggestions about all things related to design. You provide it with screenshots of your pages and it will give you great advice. Or tell it you’re planning a website featuring Instant Pot recipes, and it will give you the perfect color scheme and several home page designs from which you choose.

A poorly designed site can annoy, upset, sadden, scare, confuse, or otherwise disturb your visitors in many ways. So because most of us don’t know color theory or typeface variations, professional designers take these and other factors into account and create themes that look good.

When you first install WordPress, it comes with a default theme created by WordPress itself and named after the current year: Twenty Twenty, Twenty Twenty-Four, and so on. However, you can install a different theme if you wish. Some themes are free, and some aren’t. But as with plugins, choosing a freemium theme can prevent problems like poor support or abandonment. So you likely won’t be forced to switch to a new theme down the road. Having to switch can be problematic, especially if your site grows large.

Themes also include options that let you customize the default site design—various typeface choices, color options, replacing the header image, and so on. These customizations are located in your Appearance > Customize screen. And, as you’ll see in chapters 6 and 7, AI can help you polish your site’s design in many ways.

Although too often overlooked by site builders, appearance is important. You want your audience to feel comfortable, even if they can’t explain why they are. There’s a reason that you don’t see the color red, or paintings of bullfights for that matter, in a doctor’s office.

1.6.7 Advanced design

The best themes become popular, and that’s a bit of a drawback. You don’t want your site to look like a clone of other sites. It should look good, but in its own way. If your site sells things, you want to choose a design that promotes what you’re marketing. If you’re offering a site about gardening, how can you use photos and videos to best advantage? In other words, after you’ve chosen a theme, customize it. What does your particular audience expect to see? In what ways can you make the site look unique and in service of the site’s goal?

Finally, if design isn’t your thing. If you don’t feel confident about modifying your site’s look, no problem. You likely have a friend who knows their way around design. Opposites attract. And, as always, AI knows all about the history of design, as well as fully understanding contemporary trends.

1.6.8 Fine-tuning design the easy way

Cascading style sheets (CSS) is a computer language that allows you to polish the appearance of a site by overruling the theme’s style defaults. In this way, you can fine-tune your site’s design, so it looks more harmonic with your topic and also looks more unique.

For example, if your theme’s body text is Garamond by default, you can use CSS to override that theme default and display your paragraphs in Roboto or Verdana. You gain pretty much complete control of all things visual on your site. You can even make all the text in the site blue, change the font style on a single page, or display a single word in Roboto and leave all the other text in Garamond. In other words, you can modify and polish your site in highly specific, granular ways. This frees you from any design constraints imposed by the theme.

Although CSS is a computer language, you don’t need to learn it. There’s an excellent WordPress plugin, YellowPencil, that generates CSS elements for you in a WYSIWYG (what you see is what you get) user interface. You move sliders to accomplish things such as resizing or repositioning a photo on a page. You can click in a color palette to change colors, choose a new typeface by selecting from a dropdown list, and make many other customizations. In this visual way, you can easily modify and improve the appearance of your site without having to write a single line of code. YellowPencil knows what CSS code is required to achieve the results you’re after.

In addition to YellowPencil, there are a few other quality CSS code-generator plugins you might want to look at, CSS Hero in particular. But in chapter 7, we’ll spend a lot of time learning to use YellowPencil. It’s the most popular. And you’ll also see how to get great computer code from AI systems.

1.6.9 Managing media

One of the important benefits of online communication is that you can add various kinds of media. A site can be improved with the following additions:

  • Images

  • Audio

  • Video

  • Documents such as PDF, Word, Excel, PowerPoint, and so on

  • Interactive elements such as rotating image galleries and Google maps

  • Social media content

  • Animation

A frequent mistake in web design is the overuse or misuse of media. Contemporary site design calls for moderate, gentle animation; images that contribute to the site’s purpose (rather than serve as mere decoration.) Also, it’s considered rude to have audio and video start playing automatically all by themselves. The visitor turns them on if they wish to hear or see them. We’ll cover all the bases related to the effective, contemporary use of online media in chapter 8.

1.6.10 Setting up a blog page

A blog is a special, unique page on a website that contains messages (called posts), usually displayed in reverse-chronological order. The blog page can be a way to interact with your audience, a way to post the latest news on your site’s topic, or a customer-service feature that answers questions. Many blogs don’t permit comments from the audience because it can become time-consuming to vet all the incoming messages. But we’ll look at ways to automate the approval process. For example, you can make a list of forbidden words (“dumb”, “s***”, “hate”, etc.). If a message arrives at your site containing any of these words, it gets automatically thrown in the trash, never to be published in your blog.

To retain an audience, a blog should be frequently updated with new content, either text or media.

Although most blogs are primarily textual, some are focused on art (artlog), videos (vlog), or music. Microblogging is another type of blogging featuring very short posts, à la X (formerly known as Twitter).

Over the years, however, it’s become evident that there are several ways to mess up a blog and drive visitors away—or fail to attract them in the first place. In chapter 9, you’ll see how to build and manage a blog that contributes to, not works against, achieving your goals for your site.

1.6.11 Making money

Chapter 10 is all about monetization. Websites can be a source of income in a variety of ways, including the following:

  • Placing pay-per-click ads (links) on your site. When someone clicks a link that takes them to a page on Amazon’s (or another merchant’s) site, you earn a commission.

  • Using affiliate marketing earns you a commission if someone actually buys something after clicking the link on your site that takes them to a product.

  • Selling your own products through an e-commerce plugin such as WooCommerce.

  • Getting paid for reviews or other content about a product. This is similar to owning a billboard that businesses rent.

  • Walling off a premium area within your site that people must pay to subscribe to before they’re allowed in to see the content.

  • Providing a service such as consultation, site design, or marketing.

  • Fundraising by adding a donation plugin such as the Easy PayPal Donation.

These approaches aren’t mutually exclusive; you can combine them. But monetization must be handled with care, or you can annoy and shrink your audience. Ensure that you don’t load your site with popup or slide-in animated ads or use sketchy tactics like getting paid for positive reviews.

1.6.12 Optimizing your site for AI and search engine ranking

To attract an audience, you want to rank high in search engines and now AI systems. After typing in what they’re looking for, people don’t often go drilling five down in Google’s list of links past the first page.

This is even more true of AI searches that often display only one or a few links (and sometimes none), not multiple pages of links. AI systems’ designers like to say that whereas Google gives you a list of sites you have to visit to search for the answer you’re looking for, AI, by contrast, gives you the answer itself.

You want a link to your site to show up prominently when a potential visitor searches for your topic. This means you want to impress AI systems, Google, and Bing when one of their spider bots comes to visit you, judge you, and report back to the mothership.

AI, Google, and other searchers have a list of things they don’t like at all! For example, if they find redundancy, ambiguity, or imprecision, they subtract points right then. So you, as your site’s administrator, need to pay attention to the quality of your content, site navigability, accuracy, keeping current, and overall excellence. In other words, because AI, Google, and others strive to provide the best possible search results, ensure that your site is convincingly the best possible answer to someone’s question or need. Don’t try gaming these systems. They know the tricks. Provide quality instead.

Achieving a high rank isn’t easy anymore, and paying attention to SEO is essential if you plan to grow an audience (we’ll dive deep into SEO in chapter 11). And now, you also need to pay attention to AI optimization (AIO). Google, Bing, and other search systems are rapidly evolving to incorporate AI into their processes. The days of seeing a long list of links are over.

Estimates state that more than 80% of people who go online use Google three times a day on average. Google and AI are the pathways to your site for most of your potential new visitors.

In the early pre-Google days, search engines were rather crude. It was pretty easy to get a high rank. For example, you could be listed first in the results when someone searches for a classic car site if you just included the phrase classic cars 400 times. And the closest runner-up site used the phrase less often.

But then Google came along and changed the game. Google’s primary initial breakthrough (that went beyond multi-word, primitive, quantitative ranking algorithms) was to imitate the way that academic papers are ranked. The number of other papers that cite your paper determines your rank. This is qualitative judgment. The idea is that a paper must have importance and quality if it’s frequently cited by other scholars.

Similarly, search systems look at how many other sites link to your site (called backlinks). The systems also consider the quality of the sites that are backlinking to your site. Plus, Google and AI now make dozens of other kinds of judgments as well.

Thanks to AI and deep data mining, today’s search systems are quite sophisticated. Some say that their critiques are now of sufficient quality to be equivalent to the judgments that expert human critics would make. Here are some strategies commonly used to please the searchers’ bots:

  • Keywords—Using the same terms that someone would type into Google when looking for a site on your topic

  • Optimization—Modifying your site to make it closer to what searchers expect to find in a useful site—in the site’s title, headlines, navigation, images, and other elements

  • Content—Ensuring that your site is full of well-written, valuable content useful to your target audience

  • Links—Finding ways to increase the number of backlinks

  • Speed—Testing your site’s page-load speeds and overall performance and adding a plugin that can speed things up if necessary

1.6.13 Using analytics to build and keep your audience

Website analytics means learning how to improve your site by understanding the behavior of your visitors and measuring the site’s overall performance. You can use analytic plugins to give you potentially valuable data on the following:

  • How many visitors come to the site

  • Where visitors came from before visiting you

  • Which pages on the site people looked at (and for how long)

  • Which links they clicked

  • The pathways they followed through the site

  • Where they were when they left your site

  • What, if anything, they searched for within the site

In sum, analytics data lets you know what design elements and content to improve. They can also report on the effectiveness of your marketing efforts if your site is monetized.
