8 Mastering multimedia

This chapter covers

  • Choosing the appropriate number, size, and type of images to display
  • Knowing where to store media files
  • Making sure your site is accessible to all visitors
  • Paying attention to legal matters such as plagiarism, permission, and attribution
  • Managing media when using the block editor

Multimedia is one of the advantages that websites have over print media. Although books and magazines can display images, they can’t include audio or video. In almost every site, you want to add at least some video and certainly images. Audio is optional, and relatively less common.

The numbers support this. Studies show that website content with good, relevant visuals gets 94% more views! (See https://mng.bz/av4x for statistics about how effective visuals are.) You can also read what Forbes magazine says about visual learning and how younger generations in particular base purchasing decisions on images at https://mng.bz/MDWE.

The majority of people claim to be visual learners. In this chapter, we’ll explore the best ways to add relevant pictures, audio, and video to your site to bring it alive and give your visitors a reason to stay awhile.

8.1 Displaying the right amount of media

Of course, you want to be judicious in your use of images or other media. Use too few, and the site becomes static and might look forbiddingly full of gray text. Too many, and your audience will likely become distracted and perhaps annoyed because the pages lack focus.

There’s no magic number though. You can’t just aim for, say, five per page. There’s a range of the number of media you should use, depending on the site’s subject matter. An artist displaying works in a portfolio site can include many images, and so can an e-commerce site featuring a catalog of goods. A lawyer’s site—attempting to avoid the appearance of frivolity—can be expected to display fewer visuals.

8.1.1 Testing page-load speed

Page-load speed is another consideration. Too many images can slow down your site. And you need to take into account that some of your visitors will have slow internet connections. To test your page-load speeds, follow these steps:

  1. Temporarily deactivate any coming soon or under construction plugins such as SeedProd.

  2. Go to the Pages > All Pages screen and then click a page’s title.

  3. Copy that page’s permalink (right above the toolbars).

  4. Go here to submit the permalink for the page-load test: https://pagespeed.web.dev.

  5. Paste the permalink into the URL field and click the Analyze button.

8.2 Avoiding inappropriate images, audio, or video

In this context, inappropriate media can mean several things:

  • Doesn’t match the style of your site

  • Doesn’t fit in with the purpose of your site

  • Downright vulgar or otherwise offensive

  • Merely decorative rather than advancing your site’s story

  • Too large or too small

  • Low quality or low resolution

  • Lacks alternative text descriptions (Alternative Text in the Image Details screen or Alt Text in the Edit Gallery screen).

Note Alt text is used by text readers to describe the image to people who have visual challenges, or to provide an alert if the image isn’t available for display (images only). It’s also used by search engines.

  • Lacks captions or transcripts

  • Video or audio that starts playing by itself

  • Video or audio that is frenetic, distracting, or otherwise annoying

  • Isn’t permissible to use because the media was created by someone else

  • Isn’t properly formatted or sized for internet display (discussed shortly)

Note You shouldn’t use media if it contains someone’s face or license plate number. It’s said that the reason Google blurs these in its Google Earth app is that somebody got into a lot of trouble when they parked their car in front of a notorious house.

The Media Library isn’t infinite. You need to understand its several limitations, and the solutions to those restrictions. Let’s next consider the issue of media file size. Put briefly: avoid uploading large files into your WordPress Media Library.

8.3 Storing video on YouTube

Individual images (and also text media such as PDF or DOCX files) are comparatively small files. So it’s usually not a problem to store them in your Media Library. However, the Media Library has a file size limit. How big depends on your hosting service (256 MB is typical).

By contrast, video files are enormous. Here’s why: an average website image is around 150 KB. But a 5-minute video is 9,000 times larger. Typically, a video has a frame rate of 30 frames per second, in other words, 30 images per second. So, a 5-minute video is roughly the equivalent of 9,000 images, or 1,350 MB. (This does vary based on the specs and type of the image or video, but obviously video eats up memory fast.)

Storage on WordPress hosting services’ servers isn’t free. Limitations are placed on how much storage you’re allowed. There can also be maximum file size limits. So, unless you have a small site with only a few brief videos, avoid storing videos in your Media Library.

Because they’re huge, videos also consume significant bandwidth and server resources. Your site’s performance can be affected in that way as well.

Videos can be compressed. This helps improve page-load speed and smooth video playback. The Media Library, however, might not have access to sophisticated video optimization, such as managing compression (this depends on your hosting service). But it’s usually best for all the reasons previously mentioned to store videos off-site in a third-party video platform such as YouTube. After they’re stored there, you embed (paste) a video player in a page on your site. Then, when a visitor clicks the Play button in the player, YouTube streams your video to your site’s page for your audience to view it.

YouTube lets you store videos for free! And, aside from relieving your hosting service from storing and delivering video, YouTube storage gives you these additional benefits:

  • Efficient, smooth streaming

  • Advanced optimization and compression

  • Management of multiple video formats

  • An easy way to embed videos into your site

  • A customizable video player

8.3.4 Modifying the video player

In the Text tab of a text box on the Edit Page, you can view and modify the video player’s HTML embed code if you wish. Here’s sample embed code that includes attributes you might want to modify or, in the case of autoplay, just delete altogether. Most people don’t like it when video or audio automatically starts playing. They want to decide when to turn media on. So, delete autoplay and the semicolon that follows it:

<iframe width="560" height="315" src=https://www.youtube.com/embed/eM6Axxx4WlLLwI
title="YouTube video player" frameborder="0"
allow="accelerometer; autoplay; gyroscope;
</iframe>

Here’s a description of the tags and their settings if you want to consider changing them as well:

  • The <iframe> HTML element is used to embed another document or web page within the current HTML document. It can be used for ads or, in our case, embedded video.

A little HTML terminology

In HTML, code like this enclosed in greater-than and less-than symbols (<>) such as <iframe> and </iframe> are called tags, and they describe what kind of content they enclose and sometimes include additional specifications such as size, position, or typeface. The open and closing tags, plus what’s contained between them, are an HTML element. And when you see an =" symbol within an element, such as title="Our Christmas Party", that additional information about an element is called an HTML attribute.

  • The width and height attributes describe in pixels the size and shape of your embedded video player.

Warning Specifying width and height can make the video player unresponsive (meaning it wouldn’t correctly resize to a display like a cellphone screen). So if you do choose to specify the player’s size, be sure to test it by looking at your web page on a cellphone, tablet, and monitor. Your site’s theme has the task to handle responsiveness, but if it doesn’t do a good job, here’s a CSS fix: https://mng.bz/gaYZ.

  • The src attribute is the URL address of the content that will be fetched and displayed. In this case, it’s a YouTube video.

  • The title attribute isn’t displayed. Visually challenged people use screen readers that read this attribute (and other page content) out loud. So the title attribute should describe the video.

  • Frameborder has only two values: 0 displays no frame around the contents, and 1 does.

  • The allow attribute can include multiple properties that are actually a list of permissions:

    • accelerometer allows the embedded content to access the device’s accelerometer, that, as you might expect, detects and measures acceleration of a cellphone or tablet. It reads any acceleration applied to the device along all three axes. In other words, if a table is viewed in portrait mode, and then the user turns it 90 degrees, the accelerometer detects this movement, and the screen adjusts to landscape mode. It becomes wider than it is tall.

    • autoplay starts the video running when the page is displayed, which is usually annoying to users. Let the user determine when to start the show. Some browsers actually block auto-playing. And it can impact accessibility because screen readers might be reading other content that auto-playing audio would suddenly interfere with. The solution is to not include the autoplay property in your code.

    • gyroscope is hardware in cellphones and tablets that is sensitive to twisting or rotation. It detects spatial orientation and improves the accuracy of the accelerometer. Working together, these devices can cause the software to redraw the screen to a new aspect ratio, such as changing from landscape to portrait mode.

8.4 Using the right image sizes

Images taken by cellphones and other modern cameras are very high resolution, which means large file sizes that use up storage space quickly. Hi-res is good when you want to crop the image to display only a detail, for example. If there are tons of pixels in the image, then even when cropped, the result remains sufficiently sharp.

But take a look at figure 8.7. It shows what happens when you crop a low-res image. This is called pixelization—unfocused, boxy, and diagonal edges are stair-stepped.

Figure 8.7 When you crop a low-res image, the result can be serious degradation like this pixelization.

However, huge hi-res image files when displayed in websites can slow page load times. This not only annoys people with less than swift internet speeds, but can also reduce your site’s quality score with Google and AI systems. The cure is to reduce the size of hi-res images. Large, super-high-resolution images aren’t any sharper in a web page than reasonably smaller images with a lower pixel density. So, the only value of working with a high-res image is if you want to crop it, and thus reduce the file size.

Two factors contribute to image: resolution and image file size. Resolution means how densely the image’s pixels (points of light) are packed. A pixel—a small (usually) colored dot—is the smallest unit of measurement for screen displays. Resolution is often measured in pixels per square inch (PPI), that is, how many pixels are packed into a screen inch.

File size represents how large the image is (usually measured by the number of pixels horizontally multiplied by the number vertically). For example, a 1,080-pixel HD video image measures 1,920 × 1,080 pixels, so it has more than 2 million pixels.

Here are some typical types of images used in websites, and the recommended size and aspect ratio (shape) for each type. These figures are for images saved to disk using the .jpg file type (other formats, such as .png files, can be quite a bit larger):

  • Background images (or other full-screen images)—1,920 × 1,080 pixels with an aspect ratio of 16:9 (400 KB file size)

  • Hero image—1,280 × 720 pixels with an aspect ratio of 16:9 (200 KB file size)

  • The site’s banner (header) image—1,600 × 600 pixels with an aspect ratio of 5:3 (200 KB file size)

  • Ordinary page or post (blog) images—1,200 × 630 pixels with an aspect ratio of 3:2 (180 KB file size)

  • Logo—For a rectangular logo, 250 × 100 pixels with an aspect ratio of 2:3 (30 KB file size)

The hero image is the first image a visitor sees. It should be carefully chosen to represent the site’s content. It introduces the site to its potential audience. The hero is sometimes a banner or header image. It can even be full screen.

The resolution for any of these image types should be 72 PPI. Images on computer screens don’t need a higher resolution. Computer screens display between 72 and 100 PPI. Today’s cellphone cameras take pictures at 300 PPI and even higher densities. Going beyond 72 PPI won’t improve how images look onscreen in a website but will increase file size.

For example, the default size and resolution of a picture taken by an iPhone 12 is 4,032 × 3,024 pixels and 460 PPI. That’s about 12 times too big for use as a typical web page image. The file size of a .png file type of this image can be around 31 MB versus the file size of a .png file for a typical web page image, which can be around 3 MB.

So before uploading them into WordPress, you’ll want to prepare images at the needed resolution and the file size appropriate to their purpose on the site (logo, ordinary image, full-screen, and so on). Because print media calls for a much higher resolution than monitors or other device screens (usually 300 PPI or higher), you’ll normally be reducing both the PPI and the image size when preparing images for display on your site. I’ll show you how to resize images and adjust their PPI shortly.

Note that those previous specs are guidelines, so they can vary somewhat depending on the use case and the type of image. But reduction is usually called for. And also consider that enormous image files impact page load speed, and thus AI systems’ and Google’s opinions of the quality of your audience’s user experience can suffer.

WordPress doesn’t automatically compress images when you upload them to the Media Library. It saves a one-to-one copy of your image, but can also generate other sizes of it: thumbnail, medium, large, and full size (the original size). It doesn’t reduce the resolution, however.

WordPress includes several tools, such as a cropping tool, to manipulate images. However, dedicated photo-editing applications are better suited for quality image manipulation, such as resizing. Prepare your images in Photoshop or an alternative application before uploading them into WordPress. If you don’t have Photoshop, you can get a free, and excellent, alternative by safely downloading Paint.NET or GIMP from https://ninite.com.

Tip Although it’s possible to superimpose text on images in WordPress using CSS, a better and more efficient solution is to just use the text tool in a photo-editing application. It’s easier, and the results are more predictable when your site is viewed on other screen sizes.

Photo-editor applications include tools to resize and modify the resolution of images. In Paint.NET, for example, choose Image > Resize (Ctrl-R). Leave the Maintain Aspect (shape) checked, and then type in the desired width. The height automatically changes to preserve the shape. Then, reduce the resolution to 72. 8.5 Positioning media effectively

It matters where you display images in your pages. If you aren’t experienced in design, you’ll want to ask for help from an acquaintance who is. Or submit a copy of your page and ask AI’s opinion about image placement.

You can also learn by looking at award-winning websites. Here are two that won design contests:

  • Airbnb effectively displays a catalog-style image layout: www.airbnb.com.

  • This New York Times “Snow Fall” article effectively integrates videos, a slideshow, and fade-in transitions for the photos. Notice that images are consistently on the right side: https://mng.bz/5gN4.

Here’s a checklist of media placement and layout best practices to consider (if you’re going to ask AI for its suggestions, submit this list to the AI first):

  • Unless you’re displaying a catalog or a set of thumbnail images, it’s best to vary their size, usually based on their importance.

  • Every image should serve a purpose beyond mere decoration. Media should be relevant to what’s discussed on the page.

  • People look at the highest image or video on a page first, so ensure that those media represent the page’s topic effectively. Less important media can go further down the page. (Some site-analytics applications can generate a heat map which visually illustrates how people view your pages. It shows areas that are never, sometimes, or always viewed. More on this in a later chapter.)

  • It’s often advisable to insert images (or white space) between every two or three paragraphs to break up the text.

  • Don’t crowd media. Provide a sufficient white space frame around each image or video. However, some designs work by flowing the text around an image or video.

  • Be consistent. If you display your images on the left side in most pages, you should have a good reason for not placing them there in all the pages.

  • Check how the media layouts work when viewed on a cellphone screen.

  • Locate media next to any reference to them in the text.

  • Provide captions.

  • Ensure that media doesn’t distract from important navigation features such as the site menu.

  • Logos are usually placed on the top-left side, sometimes on the right, but rarely in the center.

8.6 Providing accessibility

Given that a significant percentage of your audience faces various kinds of challenges accessing internet content, you want to build a site that is easy for everyone to use and enjoy. Plus, it’s clearly the right thing to do.

In this context, accessibility means ensuring that your site is efficiently usable by everyone, no matter what their impairment:

  • Visual impairments, including low vision, color blindness, or general blindness

  • Hearing issues such as reduced hearing or deafness

  • Motor or dexterity challenges that affect using a mouse or keyboard

  • Cognitive disabilities that affect comprehension and focus

Your goal should be to remove obstacles that prevent access to your content and functionality. People should be able to understand, visualize, navigate, interact, and even contribute messages if you have a two-way blog page.

The World Wide Web Consortium works to develop standards for the internet. Among their efforts are the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) accessibility best practices and standards. They list four principles, often referred to as POUR:

  • Perceivable—Information can’t be conveyed only through sensory characteristics such as size, shape, sound, or visual location because some of your visitors can’t perceive those elements.

  • Operable—Components such as keyboard navigation work for all users.

  • Understandable—Content is clear and easily comprehended.

  • Robust—The site works across a wide range of browsers and devices.

The Americans with Disabilities Act

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) is similar to WCAG, but there are some differences. ADA merely requires site accessibility, without specifying any technical standards. It just refers you to the WCAG standards. You can be sued or penalized via ADA, but WCAG has no legal authority. So far, lawyers for plaintiffs’ complaints about ADA violations are largely limited to “places of public accommodation” meaning a motel, bakery, theater, store, gas station, lawyer’s office, state and federal sites, and so on.

If your site supports a brick-and-mortar store, for example, you could be sent a warning letter or a summons if the site is inaccessible in some way. By contrast, if your site is about how to take good pictures or bake muffins, it’s quite unlikely you would be targeted. ADA enforcement hasn’t been completely consistent, however. So, this is another reason to make your site accessible. To sum up: you’re at greatest risk if you’re selling something via your site or have an associated brick-and-mortar business.

8.6.1 Why you should do it

Obviously, it’s good to make provisions for the many people who can profit from your efforts to be inclusive. In addition to making the right moral choice, you also enjoy these benefits:

  • Reach a wider audience, opening your content to more users

  • Comply with web accessibility laws and thus avoid potential legal action

  • Enhance your brand image by showing that you care about inclusivity

  • Allow people to use assistive technologies such as screen readers, voice control, switches, and other tools

  • Improve search engine optimization (SEO) ranking (it’s said that Google, Bing, and AI search engines rank your site higher if it’s accessible)

8.6.2 Finding an accessible theme

Some themes are designed with accessibility built in. One feature to look for in a theme is whether it employs Accessible Rich Internet Applications (ARIA) features called roles. This approach employs HTML attributes to make things easier for a screen reader to clarify the structure and various zones of your site. HTML version 5 includes many such elements. If you prefer an efficient frontend for axe DevTools that also generates a detailed Microsoft Excel report, add the LERA extension to your browser: https://mng.bz/nRQv.

Here are some things to look for in a theme:

  • Easy navigation via keyboard (no mouse interface required) for people who struggle with typing.

  • Responsiveness displaying your site clearly on small screens for those in your audience with vision issues.

  • ARIA roles to expand HTML for people with disabilities, especially those using assistive technologies such as screen readers, custom controls, and magnifiers.

  • Color contrast options for visitors with low vision.

  • A captioned tab order that makes traversing pages by pressing Alt-Tab clear, so a screen reader can identify each tab by the caption description.

  • Captions for any embedded videos or audio so a visitor can decide whether to play that content.

  • Closed captions for videos to make video content accessible to deaf and hard of hearing individuals via a textual representation of the audio. They also benefit individuals with learning disabilities or attention deficits to maintain their concentration.

  • Alt Text descriptions of images for blind and visually impaired individuals using screen readers.

  • Text resizing (zoom) for visually challenged visitors.

  • Skip links.

Definition Skip links are helpful for screen readers and keyboard-only users. These specialized links aren’t visible until someone starts tabbing through the various sections in a page (similar to filling out a web page form, where pressing tab takes you to the next field in the form). With this feature, people can jump over content they’re uninterested in, such as banners, long menus, or repeated content like the site title. Skip links can be kept hidden from mouse users who aren’t using a screen reader by adding CSS code like this: position: absolute; left: -999px. Examples include Skip to Menu and Skip to Next Headline.

8.7 Understanding permissions, attribution, and other legalities

The law is vast, evolving, and sometimes contradictory. But it can’t be ignored. Do you need to take steps to copyright your original content in your site? Do you need to avoid using content that’s not original? No and yes, apparently.

I’m not qualified to provide legal advice. What follows is what my research has revealed, but if you need advice on steps you need to take to remain within the law, do contact a lawyer. Please don’t consider what follows as legal advice. It’s merely a starting point for your own research, or to provide you with questions to discuss with a lawyer, or to caution you about things like plagiarism.

I’ve read that when you create new text, art, music, or any kind of original content, it’s automatically copyrighted. It’s covered as soon as you write it down, draw it, paint it, or otherwise make it tangible. And publishing a website clearly makes the content tangible. The automatic copyright gives you exclusive ownership. You have the sole right to distribute, display, reproduce, and grant reproduction permissions to the works. Work by others is also copyrighted this way. You can’t just lift their work and paste it into your site. (As often in the law, there are some exceptions.)

If that seems clear, it isn’t. I’m doing my best with this topic, so leave me alone. 🙂

Sometimes, copyright is only partial. For example, if someone publishes a pizza recipe, then their instructions, their description of the process, and any anecdotes or narratives are copyrighted. But the actual ingredients—dough, cheese, tomato sauce, pepperoni, onions—can’t be copyrighted. Nor can facts be copyrighted, such as the location of London or the date of the moon landing. These are considered absolute facts about space and time, regardless of what Einstein said. Also, different countries have different copyright and trademark laws.

Courts exist because legal issues can be interpreted in various ways. For example, now and then, someone brings a case against a musician because there is some similarity between their song and a later song (usually one that made a lot of money). The accusation must be adjudicated because there are degrees of similarity. If it’s too similar, the complainant cashes in. But given that the Western music scale only has 12 notes and only a limited number of rhythms that make any sense, similarities are bound to occur. Often these music copyright cases fail for the same reason that a painter can’t copyright a color. And as any creative person knows, one is influenced by the work of others.

It’s interesting that some people complain that AI paintings are inspired by previous human paintings. Well, most human painters also derive inspiration from existing styles. Until photography came along, new styles were quite rare. But when cameras could do a better job at realism, artists responded with an explosion of new styles, many silly or bizarre. These styles were often more a reaction against realism than an effort to find new ways to display beauty in visual form.

8.7.1 Mastering paraphrasing

Most authors research and learn from what others have published. But you can’t then just totally copy and paste someone else’s text. You must paraphrase it, which means putting things into your own words. You can repeat the facts, but not how they were worded by the other writer.

A new tool is emerging via AI—paraphrasers. You feed them somebody’s text, and they rewrite it, retaining the meaning of the text without simply replicating it. QuillBot (https://quillbot.com/) is a popular one. They say they have 50 million users. That application includes several features such as a plagiarism checker. I asked QuillBot to paraphrase the first half of an earlier paragraph in this section that starts with Courts exist . . .. Here’s QuillBot’s revision:

Because there are numerous ways to understand legal matters, courts are necessary. For instance, occasionally someone files a lawsuit against an artist over the similarities between their song and a subsequent song (typically one that was quite successful). There are varying degrees of similarity, thus the accusation needs to be evaluated.

Another popular paraphraser is Wordtune (www.wordtune.com). Like QuillBot, it has additional capabilities, such as allowing you to select a writing style (casual, formal), specify sentence length by shortening or expanding sentences, and others. Several reviewers say that Undetectable (www.undetectable.ai) is the best at these tasks.

8.7.2 Further legal questions

What if you took a video of your friends, but someone in the video prefers not to be seen on the internet? Did you get written permission from everyone at the gathering who is in the video? And anyone in the background as well?

To be on the safe side, you might want to have a lawyer draw up a permission form for photos or videos you use on your site. And get signatures from anyone visible in these media.

Can you embed someone else’s video on your site? Even if you get permission from the creator, what if there’s a copyright violation within their video. Maybe there’s a pro football game on TV in the background? Are you then secondarily liable?

You can’t just attribute or credit the owner of a video. Saying who created the video doesn’t necessarily free you from needing permission in all cases. Nolo provides a good tutorial on permissions: https://mng.bz/vKNx.

But YouTube does provide a legal way for you to embed others’ videos. YouTube says you can do it if the person who uploaded the video didn’t turn off the YouTube embedding option. Recall that earlier in this chapter, you uploaded a video to YouTube, and then clicked the Share option (refer to figure 8.5). An embed option was among several ways to share the video. So, if you see that embed icon (refer to figure 8.6), the owner of the video has implicitly granted permission to embed their video in websites. The embed option can be turned off, but you have to take these steps:

  1. Set up a YouTube Content Manager for yourself.

  2. After signing in, click your favicon (profile icon) in the upper-right corner of the YouTube screen.

  3. Click YouTube Studio in the dropdown list.

  4. Click Content.

  5. Click to select the video you want to manage.

  6. Click Settings on the left side.

  7. Click Permissions.

Note Here is what YouTube itself has to say on this topic: www.youtube.com/about/copyright/fair-use/.

8.7.3 Finding free media

Another way to avoid copyright infringement problems is to use “free” media. Several online sources offer stock photos, music, and videos that you don’t have to pay for or get permission to use. However, you want to read their terms of service or licensing requirements because free doesn’t always mean free from restrictions.

For example, stock photo sites might allow you to use their content as long as you include attribution, or provide a link back to their site. Other free content can only be used for noncommercial purposes.

Nonprofit organization Creative Commons was set up with two purposes in mind: to allow content creators—video makers, photographers, artists, and musicians—to retain their copyrights while still permitting others (like us) limited use of the creations. The organization offers several variations of licenses that creators can choose from, granting various kinds of specific permissions for others to use their works. Some such licenses require that any derivative works (variations on the original) be in turn licensed under the same terms. Others forbid derivative works entirely. Some require attribution or noncommercial use only.

Several free content collection sites such as Flickr, Wikimedia Commons, Pexels, and Vimeo allow you to search for content based on which kind of Creative Commons licenses the content creators have chosen. This approach is more efficient for you because you don’t need to contact creators individually to seek their permissions. Following are some of the best free media resources:

Summary

  • A site without media looks pretty bare. But a site with media of the wrong size, or positioned in the wrong place, or otherwise inappropriate looks sloppy and amateurish. Ensure your media plays well with the other elements in your pages and is harmonic with the site’s overall purpose. In this chapter, you explored tricks of the trade to manage media effectively.

  • Video files are enormous, and audio files aren’t all that small either when compared to text files. Even a standard definition video is roughly 20 times larger than an audio file of the same duration. So, your best tactic is to embed these media rather than trying to house them in your Media Library. Page load times will be faster, and you won’t burden your site’s database with unnecessary file storage.

  • For several reasons, you want to make your site inviting and accessible to people facing diverse challenges—whether auditory, visual, or other. You can use various tools to both analyze and improve site accessibility.

  • The law has something to say about using other people’s media or text content. And you don’t want to get a letter from a lawyer because you neglected to get necessary permissions or failed to display required attributions. Several sites offer free-to-use media, but you need to read their licensing requirements because free doesn’t always mean do whatever you want.

  • So far, the WordPress block editor’s media blocks aren’t the best choice when it comes to adding media to your site. But there are excellent alternatives such as EmbedPress.
